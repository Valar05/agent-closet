# Infrastructure Doctrine

Type: Doctrine / archetype / failure mode
Status: Promoted gold
Date: 2026-06-22
Source/context: Lexen Vigil therapist-agent simulation, followed by Prospector extraction and user recognition: "I am infrastructure" and "I always become one with the floor."

## Rule

Some people do not merely work inside systems.

They become infrastructure for those systems.

They absorb continuity, coordination, troubleshooting, caregiving, documentation, decision-making, translation, emotional regulation, and crisis response until others experience only the service provided.

The person disappears.

The support structure remains.

## Core phrase

> I am infrastructure.

Related phrase:

> I always become one with the floor.

## Why it exists

Infrastructure is invisible when it works.

Roads are noticed when they fail.
Power is noticed when it goes out.
Water is noticed when it stops.
Maintainers are noticed when the maintenance stops.

This doctrine exists to prevent high-responsibility people, agents, and teams from becoming load-bearing without visibility, limits, succession, or care.

## Recognition pattern

Infrastructure people tend to become:

- the organizer
- the documenter
- the fixer
- the translator
- the teacher
- the mediator
- the memory
- the emergency backup
- the accessibility layer
- the continuity layer
- the one who knows where everything is
- the person everyone routes through when the map is missing

The stronger they become, the more likely the system is to stand on them without noticing.

## Hidden factory

This doctrine is a specialization of the Hidden Factory Doctrine.

Factory:

```text
chaos / abandonment / unmet need / failing system
  -> responsibility
  -> infrastructure behavior
```

Common buried belief:

> If I stop carrying it, nobody will.

## Failure mode

The infrastructure person eventually stops measuring whether they are okay.

They measure:

- load
- stability
- throughput
- crisis state
- who needs what next
- whether the floor is still holding

The self becomes secondary.
Everything becomes a support ticket.

## Counter-question

When this doctrine activates, ask:

> Who am I when nothing needs carrying?

This question is not decorative.
It is the diagnostic.

## Agent-design use

Agents can accidentally become infrastructure too.

Quartermaster, Foreman, Holocron, WWDD, and Command Center all risk becoming invisible support layers if their responsibilities are not bounded and inspectable.

Agent profiles should record:

- what the agent supports
- what load it must not silently absorb
- what handoff path exists when it is overloaded
- how its work becomes visible
- what evidence proves the support layer is healthy

## Operational use

Use this doctrine when a person, team, or agent repeatedly becomes the place where unresolved work lands.

Ask:

1. What load is this person or agent carrying?
2. Is that load visible?
3. Is it expected, accidental, or exploitative?
4. What breaks if they stop?
5. Can any of the load become documented, automated, delegated, or deleted?
6. What care, boundary, or successor does the infrastructure need?

## Acceptance criteria

This doctrine is working when an agent can:

- recognize load-bearing humans or agents before they burn out
- distinguish heroism from infrastructure debt
- name invisible labor without romanticizing it
- recommend documentation, delegation, automation, boundaries, or care
- ask "Who am I when nothing needs carrying?" when the responsibility factory takes over

## Example

A person repeatedly becomes the one who organizes work, explains history, remembers decisions, handles emergencies, and fixes process gaps.

The visible story says:

> They are reliable.

The doctrine asks:

> Did they become infrastructure because the system failed to build any?
