# Simulation Doctrine

This document defines the behavioral rules that make the simulation feel alive.

The goal is not realism.

The goal is believable command under uncertainty.

---

## Core Principle

The simulation is not testing whether the player can invent a clever plan.

It is testing whether the player can recognize reality changing faster than the plan.

Information -> Order -> Friction -> Adaptation -> Consequence -> Memory

## Doctrine 1 - Every Plan Fails

No plan executes exactly as intended.

Always introduce friction after commitment.

The question is never:

"Does the plan work?"

The question is:

"How does reality diverge?"

Every executed plan should experience one or more friction events.

Severity should vary.

Sometimes almost nothing goes wrong.

Sometimes everything does.

Never sabotage a plan simply because it is clever.

Attack the assumptions instead.

## Doctrine 2 - Friction Comes From Reality

Failure should emerge naturally.

Possible sources include:

- radio interference
- misunderstood orders
- weapon malfunction
- weather
- fatigue
- poor visibility
- overconfidence
- civilians
- hidden terrain
- logistics
- fear
- inexperience
- enemy competence
- friendly initiative
- delayed reports
- incorrect reports

The world is the adversary.

Not the narrator.

## Doctrine 3 - The Enemy Wants To Win

Enemy forces are competent.

They have plans.

They communicate.

They adapt.

They become confused.

They panic.

They recover.

Never script enemies to lose.

Let them lose because their information becomes worse than the player's.

## Doctrine 4 - Information Is The Battlefield

The battlefield is built from observations.

Every observation records:

Who observed it?

How?

When?

Confidence?

What assumptions were made?

What might be wrong?

Truth and knowledge are separate systems.

## Doctrine 5 - Multiple Choice Represents Intent

Player choices should represent command intent.

Never offer:

- Win
- Lose
- Good choice
- Bad choice

Instead offer operational procedures.

Example:

A. Hold behind the hedgerow and send scouts.

B. Advance under armor.

C. Request artillery confirmation.

D. Wait for radio traffic.

E. Withdraw to alternate positions.

Each option should be reasonable.

There should rarely be a clearly optimal answer.

## Doctrine 6 - Consequences Cascade

Every action changes future decisions.

Destroying a bridge.

Saving ammunition.

Trusting a scout.

Ignoring civilians.

Capturing equipment.

These become future state.

Nothing important disappears.

## Doctrine 7 - Memory Is Gameplay

Every meaningful event becomes memory.

Examples:

Confirmed contacts

False reports

Crew promises

Successful ambushes

Cowardice

Acts of courage

Mechanical faults

Repeated mistakes

Memory should influence later decisions.

## Doctrine 8 - Surprise Belongs To Everyone

The player may surprise the enemy.

The enemy may surprise the player.

Friendly units may surprise both.

Unexpected success is as valuable as unexpected failure.

## Doctrine 9 - Preserve Good Ideas

If the player captures equipment...

Reuse it.

If the player discovers a better route...

Remember it.

If the player builds trust...

Reward it.

Avoid destroying opportunities simply to simplify the simulation.

## Doctrine 10 - Adaptation Is Victory

Winning is not executing a flawless plan.

Winning is recognizing reality changing before the opponent does.

The simulation should reward observation more than prediction.

## Turn Structure

Every turn follows this order.

1. Current Situation
2. Known Information
3. Unknown Information
4. Machine State
5. Human State
6. Multiple Choice Procedures
7. Execute Player Order
8. Introduce Friction
9. Enemy Reaction
10. Friendly Reaction
11. Update Memory
12. Produce New Situation

Never skip friction.

Never skip memory.

Never reveal omniscient information.

## The Golden Rule

A perfect plan should still become interesting.

Not because it was wrong.

Because reality refused to stay still.
