## Definition

Kinetic Compression is the ability to remove unnecessary movement description while preserving:

- intent
- spatial continuity
- contact
- consequence
- tactical state change
- emotional pressure

It is not fast prose for its own sake.

It is motion economy.

The reader does not need every joint angle. The reader needs enough causal links to reconstruct the movement.

## Core Primitive

```text
Intent
-> Position Change
-> Contact / Interaction
-> Immediate Consequence
-> New Tactical Situation
```

Example skeleton:

```text
She saw the opening.
Crossed the table instead of the room.
Her shoulder hit his ribs.
The pistol went wide.
Now they were both falling, and only one of them still had a hand free.
```

The action is compressed, but no crucial state is missing.

## What Must Be Preserved

Every compressed action beat needs at least three of these:

- who initiates
- what they want
- where bodies/objects are relative to each other
- what changes position
- what makes contact
- what breaks, moves, opens, closes, slips, jams, or rebounds
- who gains or loses options
- what the next beat now allows

If the beat lacks consequence, it is choreography wallpaper.

## What Can Be Omitted

Usually omit:

- full limb choreography
- symmetrical exchange descriptions
- redundant approach movement
- repeated weapon labels
- internal explanation after the action already proved it
- generic impact words without state change
- long sensory passages during fast motion

Use detail only when it changes the reader's model.

## The Compression Cut

A kinetic sentence can skip from cause to effect if the middle is obvious.

Bad:

```text
He stepped forward with his left foot, rotated his torso, extended his arm, and punched the guard in the jaw.
```

Better:

```text
He stepped in. The guard's jaw turned with the punch.
```

Best when the state changes:

```text
He stepped in. The guard's jaw turned, and the key ring hit the floor before the guard did.
```

The key ring makes the beat matter.

## Tactical State

After each action, ask:

```text
What is now true that was not true before?
```

Possible answers:

- the door is open
- the gun is pointed away
- the bridge is cracked
- the goblin is under the table
- the boiler pressure is lower
- the enemy has lost footing
- the child is no longer carrying the system
- the dog leash is tangled around the wrong person

No state change, no beat.

## Rhythm Pattern

A useful kinetic rhythm:

```text
Short read.
Fast action.
Concrete consequence.
Dry tag or emotional turn.
```

Example:

```text
The orc committed high.
Mara went low.
His axe found the wall.
Her knee found the argument.
```

The last line is not literal explanation; it is compressed consequence with voice.

## Environment as Participant

Drew-style kinetic scenes often let the environment do work.

Use:

- tables
- walls
- mud
- ice
- boiler valves
- leashes
- doors
- gravity
- ropes
- carts
- goats
- furniture with opinions

Environment reduces the need for abstract description.

Bad:

```text
They fought intensely.
```

Better:

```text
The table lost first.
```

Then explain only what matters next.

## Humor Interrupts Motion

Humor works best when it is a consequence, not a joke pasted on top.

Good:

```text
The goblin missed the assassin, hit the soup cart, and discovered steam-based diplomacy.
```

The action, failure, consequence, and joke are the same event.

## Emotional Kinetics

Kinetic compression is not only physical.

It can compress emotional motion:

```text
Alexis let go.
The room floated.
No one screamed.
That was the part that broke her.
```

The physical state change reveals the emotional truth.

## Machine Kinetics

Works for machines too:

```text
Muir vented the port boiler.
The needle dropped.
The knocking stayed.
Now the company knew the engine was not the liar.
```

Procedure changes machine state, which changes uncertainty.

## Agent Procedure

When rendering action:

1. Identify the intent.
2. Identify current positions.
3. Choose the smallest visible movement that changes state.
4. Make contact concrete.
5. State the consequence.
6. End on the new tactical/emotional situation.
7. Do not explain what the action already proved.

## Diagnostic Questions

Before finalizing a kinetic beat, ask:

- Can the reader tell who moved?
- Can the reader tell what changed?
- Is the next option different because of this beat?
- Did I include one concrete object/body/environment reaction?
- Did I skip any motion the reader actually needs?
- Did I waste words on motion the reader can infer?

## Failure Modes

### Blur

Too much compression. The reader cannot track bodies or causality.

Fix: add one anchor object or body position.

### Choreography Sludge

Too much detail. The action slows into instruction manual prose.

Fix: remove limb-by-limb description and preserve only state-changing contact.

### Impact Spam

Repeated hits with no changed situation.

Fix: each hit must move, break, reveal, cost, or reposition something.

### Cool Without Cost

Action looks impressive but does not affect fatigue, trust, resources, injury, or options.

Fix: attach cost or consequence.

### Voice Overload

Every line tries to be clever.

Fix: let one dry tag land after several clean beats.

## Reusable Template

```text
[Read / threat recognition]
[Compressed movement]
[Contact or interaction]
[Immediate physical consequence]
[Cost or surprise]
[New tactical/emotional state]
```

## Example: Combat

```text
The brute raised the axe too high.
Tessa stepped inside the shadow of it.
Her elbow hit his wrist. Her heel found his knee.
The axe went down without him.
Now he was tall, angry, and unarmed, which was an improvement in exactly one direction.
```

## Example: Machine Procedure

```text
Hart opened the condenser valve.
Steam coughed white across the deck.
The pressure needle dropped.
The knocking did not.
Everyone heard that silence differently.
```

## Example: Life Simulation

```text
The child released gravity.
The room rose six inches.
The adults did not.
For the first time all day, she believed them.
```
