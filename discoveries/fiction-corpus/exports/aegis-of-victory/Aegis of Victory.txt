Chapter 1


The wagon creaks beneath us, its wheels groaning with every rut and rock in the dirt road. Theo sits beside me, his knees pulled to his chest, staring at the horizon like he can already see our destination. Maybe he can.


I grip the edge of the splintered bench, the rough wood biting into my palms. My parents’ voices echo in my mind, over and over, like a drumbeat I can’t escape. “Take care of your brother. Protect him. Do this for all of us.”


It should feel noble, like a mission handed down by the gods themselves. Instead, it feels like chains—tight, unyielding, and heavy.


“We’ll make it, Nikia,” Theo says suddenly, his voice soft but steady. He doesn’t look at me, his gaze fixed somewhere far ahead. “This is the right path. You’ll see.”


I want to believe him. I want to tell him he’s right, that I see the bright future he insists is waiting for us. But all I see is the emptiness of the road behind us and the towering gates of Sophion’s Ascent ahead.




---


The gates loom like the jaws of a monster, carved with intricate reliefs of philosophers and thinkers, their faces frozen in contemplation or epiphany. This is where the Logarenes live, the ones who claim to shape the world through wisdom and power.


I’ve never been this close to something so grand. The walls stretch high enough to block out the sun, their polished stone gleaming like they’ve been untouched by dirt or time. A line of people waits at the gates—some farmers, some merchants, some just like us, hoping to scrape a crumb from the Logarenes’ table.


Theo’s abilities are what brought us here. A curiosity, the philosophers called it when the traveling scholar saw him in the village. They spoke of visions, of glimpses into what might be. “A gift,” they called it. My parents agreed.


But when I look at Theo, I don’t see a gift. I see a boy too small for his age, his hands too soft to hold anything but a quill. I see the reason we’re here, the reason I’ve left everything behind.




---


We step through the gates, the crowd parting as the Logarenes’ stewards usher us forward. The air inside feels different, heavy with the scent of herbs and incense, yet impossibly clear. The paths are lined with marble statues and sprawling gardens, their perfection a stark contrast to the dust and grit of the village we left behind.


A man waits for us, his robes immaculate, his expression unreadable. His voice is clipped and formal as he speaks. “This is Sophion’s Garden, a place of cultivation and enlightenment. You will obey all rules, or you will leave.”


Theo nods eagerly. I don’t move.


The man’s gaze flicks to me, then back to Theo. “You are the boy with the visions.”


“Yes,” Theo says, his voice stronger than I expect.


The man studies him for a moment, then gestures for us to follow. I trail behind, my footsteps heavy against the polished stone.




---


Inside, the philosophers gather, their flowing robes blending with the light of the sunlit courtyard. They speak in low tones, their words lilting like a language I can’t quite understand. When they look at Theo, their eyes light with interest.


“We will take him,” one of them says, her voice calm and measured. “His abilities are… promising.”


“And me?” The words leave my mouth before I can stop them.


The philosophers exchange glances. One raises an eyebrow.


“You have no gift,” the woman says bluntly. “But there is work to be done here. The kitchens always need hands.”


I feel the weight of her dismissal like a stone in my chest. Theo looks at me, his expression tight with guilt, but I force a smile.


“It’s fine,” I say, even though it isn’t. Even though it will never be.


Weeks pass.  I do all sorts of work.  My favorite is serving.  I get to see Theo.  I can't talk much, but a few whispered words warm my heart.


I keep a journal now.  Except about my “training” from the other servants .  My body feels stronger.  Faster, more resilient.  The others say it's normal.  It's the food, the water, the air itself, or so they suppose.  They catch snatches of enlightenment from the philosophers.


Then one day I'm sent to gather mushrooms.  Theo is sent with me in a rare act of kindness.  The head cook asked for him specifically.


The forest is quiet, save for the rustle of leaves and the occasional chirp of a bird. Theo walks a step behind me, his head turning constantly, his gaze scanning the undergrowth. I can feel his unease even before he speaks.
“They don’t like us being out here,” he murmurs, his voice barely carrying over the breeze.
“Who?” I ask, though I already know the answer.
“The beasts. They’ve been… quieter.”
I shrug, adjusting the basket on my hip. “Good. Let’s hope it stays that way.”
One starving beast approaches us.  I don't know what Theo shows it, but it runs like Cerberus is after it.
________________


Theo stiffens suddenly, his hand tightening on my shoulder. “Something’s coming.”
I stop, straining to hear, and there it is—a faint sound, metallic, grating against the natural rhythm of the forest. My heart sinks as a group of warriors rounds the bend in the trail.
The man leading them is young, his patchy beard doing little to mask the sharpness of his grin. His shield catches my eye immediately—black, uneven, with veins of red twisting across its surface like bloody scars.  And the face embossed on it.  Horrific.
“Ah, hello,” the man says, his voice smooth and confident. “Would you be so kind as to direct us to where you’re from?”
My instincts scream at me to lie, to stall, to do anything but answer him honestly. Theo’s hand digs into my shoulder. “We can’t,” he whispers.
I don’t wait for permission. Grabbing Theo’s hand, I run.
________________


The chains are faster.
They lash out from the man’s shield, catching Theo around his ankle and yanking him off his feet. He hits the ground hard, his cry of pain slicing through me like a blade.
My vision blurs, then goes red, everything narrowing to the man in front of me. He hurt Theo. So he was dead.
I charge, my basket clattering to the ground behind me. Every ounce of frustration, every moment of helplessness, boils over into a red haze. My steps are quick, practiced from delivering messages, of slick tiles while I served at the bar.  The chains whip toward me, but I twist, dodge, and close the distance.
The man raises his shield, contempt flashing across his face. He swings lazily with it, as though I’m nothing but a nuisance.
The moment it touches me, the world shatters.
________________


Time stops.
The forest, the chains, the warriors—all of it freezes, locked in a gray stillness. The only thing that moves is the shield, its chains swaying faintly, drifting toward me like they’re alive.
A voice rumbles behind me, low and resonant. “Interesting. You’ve caught its attention.”
I turn, and he’s there— a powerfully built man.  I don't worship the gods, but I have no doubt he's one. His presence is a force, his frame towering and broad, wrapped in bronze armor etched with scars and symbols that pulse faintly with light. A cloak of deep crimson drapes from his shoulders, and his face is sharp, fierce, with eyes that burn like molten gold.
Two coins hover in front of his eyes, spinning slowly, their surfaces engraved with shifting constellations. Before I can focus on them, they vanish, leaving only his gaze, piercing and unreadable.
He nods toward the shield.  “Now is your chance.”
“My chance to do what, exactly?”
“Take control of your life.  Examine the chains. See how they yearn for your touch. And the face…”
I glance at the shield, and the etched face has shifted. What was once frozen in agony is now twisted into a grin, its eyes locked on me.
“Now is your chance, Nikia,” Ares says, stepping closer. His voice is both commanding and calm. “That shield was forged by a disciple of Daedalus in imitation of my glory. It has never had a proper wielder, claiming the soul of its forger. It sees something in you.  You should take it, and survive.”
“How?” I whisper, my voice trembling.
He grips his fist tightly, the motion deliberate. “Reach out.  Griefshard is yours already.  And that man is boring me now.”
________________


The chains drift closer, no longer taut. I reach out, my fingers brushing against one, and a pulse shoots through me. My mind reels, visions flickering past—constellations, unfamiliar and impossible, blazing against a backdrop of endless darkness.
Time snapped back.  The god was gone.  The shield shifted, and chained itself to me, shifting to match my size.  “Hello.” The voice sounds like rocks.  The leader looks at me with growing horror.  “It… it never talks.  What did you do?”


Hefting the shield, I don’t hesitate. It’s lighter than I expected but feels like it belongs in my hand. I don’t think—there’s no time to think. Theo is still lying on the ground behind me, the chains just now releasing his ankle.  


I’ve been in scraps before—fights in the agora when tempers flared and coins were scarce. I learned to keep my head, to stay cool even when someone threw the first punch. But when Theo’s hurt? I lose my head. Always have.  


The ground trembles as I slam the shield down, chains rattling like a beast’s warning. The leader rolls away, his eyes narrowing as the shield pulses. He’s not a master Logothete—I can tell by the way his stance falters for a moment—but he’s still more trained than I am. His coin glows faintly over his eye, its blue sheen brightening as he regains his confidence.  


“You think that will save you?” he sneers, raising his spear.  


Before I can respond, Griefshard reacts. The chains explode outward, moving faster than I can follow. The men behind him don’t even have time to scream. The chains impale them, tearing through armor and flesh, their bodies crumpling to the ground like discarded dolls.  


The leader freezes, his sneer faltering as he glances back at the bloodied sand.  


The shield growls, its voice grinding like stone. “Weak.”  


I choke back the bile rising in my throat. The chains retract, their motion slow and deliberate, dragging faint shimmers from the fallen bodies back toward the shield. The face etched on its surface shifts, its mouth widening as it devours the energy.  


“Tasty,” it mutters, almost satisfied.  


I want to drop the shield, to throw it as far from me as I can. But Theo groans behind me, and the leader moves closer, his coin flaring brighter.  


---


The leader raises his spear, its bronze surface glinting with a coating of ice. He spins it effortlessly, the air around him rippling, condensing into liquid blades that hover in the air like silent predators. His power fills the space, sharp and suffocating.  


“You shouldn’t have interfered,” he says, his voice cold and measured. “But I’ll enjoy breaking you.”  


He lunges, faster than I expect for someone of his size. I barely block with the shield, the impact sending a jolt through my arm. The chains react immediately, coiling outward to strike, but his spear flashes again, slicing through one of them. The severed link melts into a pool of black liquid on the ground.  


---


I grit my teeth, adjusting my stance. The shield feels heavier now, the chains sluggish, as if they’re struggling against his power. I can see his energy clearly—the way his coin pulses, the way the blades around him sharpen with each movement.  


He’s from the Poseidon group.  Sons of the sea or some such.   I’ve heard whispers about them. They wield the sea itself, bending its force to their will, and their strength is unmatched. His power surges with each strike, the ice on his spear spreading out like frost across the ground.  


The shield growls again, louder this time: “Strong.”  


I feel it shift in my grip, the chains tightening, pulling my arm upward. It’s guiding me again, more forcefully than before. I give in, letting it direct me. The chains lash out, wrapping around his spear, but he jerks it free, the ice coating the chains and freezing them mid-motion.  


The man smirks, the glow of his coin brightening as he echoes Griefshard’s growl. “Weak,” he says, leveling his spear at me. The word is a taunt, deliberate and biting, meant to shake me.


Before I can react, a deep, guttural belch resonates through the air, as though the world itself had exhaled. A massive bolt of energy courses through me, sharp and overwhelming, like the time I stole a sip of the apothecary’s tea extract. My veins ignite, every movement suddenly faster, every strike heavier.  


The shield struggles, its grinding voice forcing out three fractured words: “Not. Souls. Aegis.”


I don’t know what it means, but the energy surging through me is undeniable. It’s potent, untamed, and mine.  


A razor-sharp blade of molten blood bursts from the edge of Griefshard, the shield’s chains rattling in approval. I feel a sharp pull behind my eye, and suddenly, a coin materializes in my vision, its surface gleaming like freshly spilled blood. My sight shifts—overlaying my normal vision is a web of glowing nexuses, points of energy in the man’s body connected by sharp, straight lines like constellations in the night sky.  


The chains beneath me spring to life, their movements spidery and precise, lifting and propelling me forward. My body is no longer my own; it’s a puppet wielded by something far more deliberate. I don’t fight it. I let the shield guide me.  


---


Our clash is fierce. His spear flashes, the ice coating it spreading like frost across the ground, while the liquid blades of condensed air dart toward me in unrelenting arcs. My shield absorbs most of the blows, the molten blade countering his strikes with calculated precision.  


But it’s a stalemate.  


“Intent,” the shield mutters, its voice sharper this time, a command rather than an observation.  


Theo stirs behind me, trying to push himself to his knees. His movement is slight, but the man notices. His coin flares, and without hesitation, he hurls a spear of ice straight at Theo.  


Something snaps inside me. I react without thought, Griefshard’s chains lashing out as one with my will. The chain intercepts the ice mid-air, wrapping around it and shattering it into harmless shards.  


A single, unified thought floods my mind: “Protect.”


The shield groans in approval, and for the first time, I feel it fully respond to me. The chains weave a barrier around Theo, cutting him off from the man’s view.  


I turn back to my opponent, the air around me crackling with energy. Was this what the relic meant?  My intent, to harm, to kill?  I'd felt it before.  Never so strong.


It’s personal now.  


---


Griefshard’s chains lash out in a furious flurry, striking from all angles, forcing him to retreat. Each strike is deliberate, not to kill, but to distract. I close the distance, my body moving faster and smoother than ever, my steps silent as I weave through the chaos.  


The man doesn’t see me until it’s too late.  


I slam into him with all the force of my killing intent. The molten blade on Griefshard roars forward, penetrating his chest with a sickening crunch. His coin flickers, the glow dimming as blood seeps onto the sand.  


For a moment, he gasps, his eyes wide with shock and pain. Then his body crumples, the chains retracting as the shield’s face shifts back into a cold, neutral stare.  


---


Theo’s voice cuts through the silence, soft and shaking. “Nikia…”  


I turn to him, the shield still humming with energy. The fight is over, but I feel Griefshard’s presence lingering, its weight heavy on my arm.  


“Not weak,” I mutter, echoing the words he used against me. The shield’s chains rattle in approval.  
Chapter 2: The Weight That Waits


We return empty-handed. No mushrooms. Just blood, and silence.


The guards at the gate don’t speak. They watch us with tight jaws and twitching fingers, their spears never quite pointing down. Word must have reached them already. About what happened in the forest. About me.


Griefshard is quiet now. The chains have coiled inward, slithering up my arm like snakes seeking warmth. The obsidian shield tightens, folding in on itself until it becomes a single jagged bracelet around my left wrist. Its surface pulses faintly, like it’s breathing. It weighs almost nothing now.


But I still feel it. Like it’s waiting.




---


Inside the monastery walls, the air is colder. The stones seem to listen. Servants who once shared scraps with me no longer meet my eyes. Some bow. Others make strange signs with their fingers, like warding off a curse. They don’t whisper aloud, but the silence says enough.


Theo hasn’t said a word since we got back. He walks beside me with his head down, pale and distracted. I keep looking at him for signs of a limp, a bruise, anything. But it’s deeper than that. His quiet has changed. It’s too still.


A summons arrives before we even reach the scullery: the philosophers want to see us.




---


They meet us in a chamber lined with scrolls and relics, golden braziers burning soft incense. Their robes gleam with the colors of thought—saffron, violet, silver-white. I wonder if any of them have ever held a weapon. If they’ve ever knelt in blood.


The lead philosopher, a woman with a voice like cold riverstone, steps forward. She doesn’t greet me.


"You are still bound to the shield."


I nod.


"Will you allow us to attempt separation?"


I hesitate—but only for a breath. Theo’s still breathing. I don’t want to kill anyone else today.


"Yes," I say, and hold out my arm.




---


The rituals begin.


First, a salve is painted on my wrist—thick with crushed lotus and the blood of an ox born under a full moon. Then chants. Relics pressed against the bracelet. A mask of mirrors placed over my eyes.


Nothing.


The bracelet does not heat or twitch. The chains do not stir.


They bring out a coin, freshly minted, glowing faintly with cultivated intent. It’s placed against the bracelet.


Griefshard growls. "Mine."


The lights flicker.


A pulse drives everyone back a step. One philosopher drops to their knees, clutching their head. Another’s coin shatters, hitting the floor with a hiss and a flicker of dying light.


"It cannot be separated," the riverstone woman says after a long pause. Her voice trembles—barely, but it’s there. "The bond is... absolute."


I lower my arm. The bracelet tightens, just slightly, like it approves.


Griefshard whispers one more word. "Stay."


I don’t know if it means itself, or me.

The Dream Becomes Real


They let me stay with Theo now.


Not because they trust me. Because they’ve seen what the shield can do. What I can do with it. There’s nothing they can do to stop me—not really. They’ve tried rituals, relics, rules.


Griefshard won’t let go. And I won’t leave Theo’s side.


They muttered about protocol, about containment, but in the end, they just nodded. I think some of them were relieved. Let the cursed girl watch the cursed boy.


So I sleep on the floor beside his bed, on a thin mat of woven straw. I pretend I don’t mind.




---


Theo starts speaking in his sleep.


At first, it’s nonsense—syllables without shape, like a child mumbling through a fever. But by the third night, I start hearing words. Names I don’t recognize. Descriptions of places that don’t exist. Spiral sky, silver stairs, the wine-dark wall...


He doesn’t remember any of it when he wakes. But he’s always tired. Pale. Distracted. His hands tremble when he holds his quill.


And sometimes—when he looks at me—I don’t think it’s him behind his eyes.




---


On the fifth night, I wake to a sound like stone crying.


Theo screams. I sit up in a heartbeat, my hand already on the shield—even in its bracelet form, it throbs against my wrist, alive and alert.


I rush to his bedside—and freeze.


The ground beneath his bed is cracked. Not chipped or broken—cracked, in a perfect spiral, like a snail shell made of marble and starlight. From the center, blue light bleeds upward, not like fire or water, but something thicker. It pulses softly. Slowly.


Theo stands in the corner, awake, eyes wide.


"It’s here," he whispers. "It followed me."


He steps toward the crack.


"Theo—no."


He stops. Barely.


"I dreamed it last week. I was walking down... down... forever. It felt like I’d already done it before." He rubs his eyes. "I think I woke up down there."


I step between him and the light. My bracelet tightens.


Griefshard mutters: "Open."




---


The Extraction Decree


They come within the hour.


Philosophers. Stewards. Armed guards pretending not to be afraid. They flood the hall outside Theo’s chamber, but none of them cross the threshold. Not while I’m still standing inside. Not while the spiral crack still pulses with that light.


Only one dares step forward—Mistress Kaedra, the same one who tried to separate me from Griefshard. Her silver sash trails behind her like a knife’s edge.


"You will leave the room," she says—not to me, but to the air between us.


I shake my head. "He’s my brother."


"He is not your concern anymore."


I raise my arm slightly. The bracelet doesn’t glow. It doesn’t need to. The memory of it is enough.


Kaedra’s lips press thin. "Then you will listen."


She gestures, and the guards lower a heavy etched tablet onto the floor outside the door. Old symbols flicker across its surface—seal-scripts, usually reserved for containing unstable coins.


Theo flinches at the sight of it.


"What is that?" I ask.


Kaedra doesn’t answer right away. She paces. Not like she’s building drama—like she’s searching for courage.


"This phenomenon," she finally says, "is not unique. The crack beneath your brother is known to us. We call it a Logos Fracture—a rift in the world’s pattern, where intent and meaning begin to... leak."


She turns back to face me.


"If left unchecked, it will grow. And it will devour."


The other philosophers murmur behind her. One of them clutches a text so tightly his knuckles pale.


"It must be closed," Kaedra says. "And to close it, we must use the source."


I feel my stomach twist. "You mean Theo."


"His coin," she says evenly. "Not him. Not directly."


"What happens to him after?"


A pause. Too long.


"He will survive. But he will be... less."


Theo finally speaks, his voice hoarse. "Less how?"


Kaedra does not answer.




---


They begin preparations at once.


A new chamber is consecrated directly above the fracture. Ritual circles are drawn. Chains not made of metal are brought from underground vaults. Incense that smells like salt and honey fills the halls.


Theo is told it’s for his safety. That the coin is hurting him. That this will ease the pressure.


He doesn’t argue. Not aloud.


But when I sit beside him that night, the look he gives me is pleading.


He wants me to say no. To tell them they’re wrong.


I want to.


But I don’t know how.


Griefshard says nothing. But it thrums. The bracelet is cold against my skin—but the pulse beneath it is growing stronger.


Something is coming.




---


Chapter 3: The Breaking Point


They bring Theo in at dawn.


He doesn’t struggle. He barely speaks. Just walks with his head down, pale as ash, his hands clenched into tiny fists at his sides.


The chamber is cold, carved from marble and veined with silver. Symbols shimmer across the floor—glyphs I can’t read, drawn in something too red to be paint. The fracture pulses beneath the stone like a second heartbeat.


I walk with him as far as they allow. When I step forward again, three guards block my path.


Mistress Kaedra raises a hand, stopping them.


"You may remain. For now."


I nod, but I don’t thank her.


Theo is guided into the circle. The philosophers chant softly. One of them holds a vessel shaped like an eye. Another unwraps a pair of delicate tongs made from black stone. Everything they do feels fragile and final, like they’re dissecting something holy.


Kaedra steps forward.


"We will extract the coin. We will seal the breach. And your brother will live."


She doesn’t say what kind of life.


Theo glances at me. Just once.


I take a step forward, but the bracelet on my arm tightens. Griefshard pulses once.


"Wait."


The voice grinds like granite.


Inside the circle, the tongs lower toward Theo’s chest.


The moment they touch him, he screams.


Not just pain—a sound that doesn’t belong to any child. It scrapes across the stone, shaking dust from the high arches. The fracture flares beneath the floor, light blazing through the cracks like lightning frozen mid-strike.


Kaedra shouts. Others scream. The glyphs flare—


And I move.


Griefshard erupts from my arm in a blink. Chains spill out like molten metal, slamming into the circle. One wraps around the tongs and snaps them in half. Another tears the seal-glyphs apart. The third lashes toward the vessel and shatters it, spilling a plume of blue fire across the floor.


Theo collapses, gasping.


The ground groans beneath him.


Then it splits.


The fracture erupts into a spiral maw of light and wind, pulling everything toward its center. Scrolls fly. Robes whip. Philosophers stumble back, shouting incantations that fail before they leave their mouths.


Theo is sliding toward the edge.


I dive after him, chains anchoring into the marble.


Griefshard screams one word:


"Fall."


I wrap my arm around Theo as the floor gives out entirely, and the world drops.


No sky. No sound. Just spiraling stars and the long, deep tunnel of the Logos Fracture yawning open to swallow us both.


And then—


We fall into the crack he dreamed.
Into the place the gods forgot.

Chapter 4: The Bacchanalia That Would Not End
We fall for a long time.
Not through air. Through meaning.
I don’t know how to explain it. The Logos Fracture isn’t a place, not really—it’s a wound. A tear in the world’s skin, and we’re inside it now. Falling through blood and breath and something colder than either.
Then we land.
Soft, but jarring. Like dropping into deep snow that smells like crushed grapes and incense.
The world here is wrong. Purple twilight curls across the sky, but there’s no sun. The clouds pulse faintly, as if drunk on their own motion. Trees groan under the weight of overripe fruit—apples with too many eyes, grapes that twitch when I look at them too long.
We're in a clearing, ringed by cracked columns and stone benches overgrown with black-stemmed vines. Statues of women with wild eyes and ecstatic mouths peer down from broken pedestals.
It might once have been a theater. Now it’s a stage for rot.
Theo coughs beside me, his face pale, his eyes darting. He opens his mouth to speak—then stops.
We both hear it.
Music.
Faint. Distant. A reedy flute and a drum made of something that doesn’t sound like skin.
I whisper, “Where is it coming from?”
Theo points without looking. “Everywhere.”
________________


We move quickly, but not carelessly. The air here pulls. With each step, it feels like something’s trying to loosen the thread that holds me together.
Theo stays close, his hand clutching a polished rock from the floor. “Just in case,” he mutters. He doesn’t say what he means.
Griefshard is still dormant, a cold bracelet on my arm. But I can feel it twitch. Like it’s sniffing.
When we pass under a twisted trellis heavy with wine-stained leaves, something whispers.
“Come dance.”
Theo freezes. His breath catches.
Three shapes emerge from the vines.
________________


They were once women.
That’s the first thought that strikes me. Human shapes—barely. Skin covered in streaks of old paint and wine. One walks like her bones don’t bend right. Another drags a broken harp behind her like a leash. The last has no eyes, only a smiling mask carved from grape bark.
________________


The masked one steps forward first.
She tilts her head, the carved bark mask creaking like old wood. Her voice is smooth and sticky, like honey left too long in the sun.
“Come dance with us.”
I glance at Theo. He doesn’t answer. Neither do I.
The second one—the harp-dragger—laughs. It’s a wet, broken sound, like wine choking on itself.
“You’ve crossed into joy’s domain,” she purrs. “There’s no need for pain here. No weight. No war. Just rhythm and rest.”
The third lurches forward, eyes wild, limbs twitching like a marionette’s. “We danced once,” she whispers. “Before the god left. Before the shield bled.”
Theo’s voice is quiet but firm. “We’re not staying.”
The masked one sways, as if to music only she hears. “You already are. Every breath you take here belongs to us. Every heartbeat hums the rhythm.”
“We’re not here to dance,” I say. “We’re looking for a way through.”
“A way through?” the harp-dragger echoes. “There is no through. Only in. Only deeper. Only wine and song and skin and forgetting.”
She turns toward Theo, fingers curling like vines. “You look tired. Wouldn’t it be easier? To let it all go? To wake up in a world without dread?”
Theo doesn’t flinch, but he swallows hard.
I step between them.
“We’re not dancing,” I say again. “Not now. Not ever.”
The music stops.
The vines tremble.
The harp is lifted like a blade.
And the maenads scream.


Maenad fight
A shriek cuts through the clearing, and the harp-dragger charges—her teeth bared, strings dragging behind her like guts. I raise my arm, willing the chains to strike, to lash, to do something—
Nothing.
Griefshard is cold. Silent.
The maenad slams into me, and I stumble back, barely raising the shield in time. The impact sends a jolt through my shoulder, but it holds.
“Move!” I shout, but the bracelet doesn’t shift. My heartbeat hammers. My mind spins.
They’re not soldiers. They’re not trying to win.
They’re trying to pull us under.
Something in me recoils. The paint, the rot, the open mouths screaming laughter—they’re wrong. Not enemies. Victims. I don’t want to kill them. I don’t want to—
Theo’s cry splits the air. One of them is nearly on him.

“I let him be erased,” Thales says. “And I told myself it was wisdom.”
He kneels.
Then looks up at me.
“You carry your pain like a blade.”
He bows his head.
“I envy you.”
________________


The forum is quiet.
Even the wind feels still.
Theo doesn’t say anything.
Not yet.
________________


We walk a while without speaking.
The forum fades behind us, swallowed by ivy and stone. The vines that twist along the path seem to ease, no longer writhing but resting, like the ground itself has exhaled.
The rod is quiet.
Griefshard is still.
But the hollowness—that's louder now.
I feel Theo glance at me more than once. I don’t look back, not yet. My hand still bleeds from the thorns. It’s starting to crust over, dark and rough. The skin aches.
It helps me remember I’m alive.
Finally, his voice comes.
Soft. Tentative.
“You didn’t kill him.”
It’s not a question.
“No,” I say.
Theo walks a little closer.
“Why not?”
I pause.
“I don’t know.”
I do. But I don’t know how to say it.
He was already broken.
He was already me.
Theo swallows. I hear it.
“I think I would've,” he says. “If I had that power.”
He doesn't sound proud of it.
“Good thing you don’t,” I say, half-smiling.
He doesn’t smile back.
He looks up at me, eyes too tired for his face.
“I wanted to take the wine,” he says. “Back at the table. I still want to. I keep thinking about it. Like… it’s waiting for me.”
I stop walking.
He does too.
“I know,” I say.
He meets my eyes.
“I don’t want to feel this much all the time.”
My throat tightens.
“I know.”
He looks down at the rod in my hand. At the thorns. At the blood.
“Does it get better?”
I don’t answer right away.
Then I crouch, pressing the rod to the earth, letting the weight rest somewhere else for a minute.
I take his hand.
And say the only honest thing I have left:
“I don’t know. But we’re going forward anyway.”
He nods.
And this time, he doesn’t let go.
