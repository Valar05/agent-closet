Momo appeared behind one sniper in a blink. The man flinched and hurled something—glass?—but it bounced harmlessly off her crocodile plate. Tetsuya didn’t watch. He didn’t need to. She had this.


Down the trail, a new group emerged—lances in hand, moving in formation. No mounts. No raptors. Just men, two lines deep.


That gave him pause.


He felt a flicker of respect.  Those lances would kill his trihorns, sure.  But the dead body would bury the one holding it.


Kill the mount. Cripple the army.


The men drove them into the earth with practiced hands. But they didn’t hold the line. They fled—fast, low, no hesitation.


The tripods clicked into place. Long shafts settled into iron rests, tips gleaming like needles.


It wasn’t a soldier’s weapon. It was a trap builder’s.


The respect vanished.


So they hadn’t overcome their fear of death. Not yet.


He would help with that.


Two clay spheres—one in each hand. He hurled them in sequence. Inhaled. Drew the world into slow focus.


Aimed.  The pyrite bold shot forth.


The first sphere ignited midair—pyro sap. It coated the second before it hit. The second burst with a hiss—beetle extract atomized. The mist bloomed into yellow fog that danced in the firelight.


Some of the men screamed, flailing at the jelly igniting on their skin.


Others froze—arms up, legs stiff, mouths open in silence.


Thwip-thwip-thwip-thwip.


Tetsuya picked off the rest with toxic bolts. One per target. Quick, brutal, precise.


Then he heard it—twin roars behind him. Familiar. Furious.


His rexes were under attack.


He whirled. The trihorns were pinned—lances ahead, something worse behind. His alpha barely moved, twitching from the impact.


He dropped from the saddle.


“Charge forward,” he ordered. “Don’t stop charging.”


The alpha hesitated, then lowered its head and lumbered toward the spikes. The rest followed.


He turned to regroup—Momo met him in mid-run, muzzle streaked red, eyes sharp.


She licked her lips.


“Summon the pack,” he said. “They’re after my rexes.”


He was not expecting what he found.


Some kind of steampunk mech was laying into his twinrexes.


It towered above them, one arm a hydraulic claw, the other a brutal chainsaw. Steam hissed from vents along its back and shoulders. As he watched, it kicked one of his rexes over with a sharp burst of pressure. The twin-headed creature rolled, roared—helpless.


Then the claw clamped around its left neck. The chainsaw roared to life.


One clean cut. Head gone.


The remaining head butted the glass canopy in futility. Volcanic glass, reinforced. Inside the cockpit, Tetsuya could make out a human silhouette—barely—through the rippling haze of heat. And something else: the interior was dense with moss. Green. Lush. A living mat.


“Clear!” he shouted.


The pack scattered. The remaining rex backed off, barely keeping out of reach. Tetsuya loaded a pyrite arrow. Aimed. Fired.


The shot landed—flaming sap splashing against the exosuit’s frame and the downed rex. Fire met metal. Mist hissed into the air. Paralytic fog.


The rex froze.


The mech didn’t.


It stood untouched in the haze, utterly unaffected.


Hermetically sealed. With an internal plant scrubber? The moss. A CO₂ converter. Brilliant. Bastards.


His bolts bounced off the plating. They’d planned for him.


He grit his teeth. Thought fast.


The steam.


It vented out the shoulders and thighs, maybe chest. The suit ran on pressure. If he could clog the vents, it might overheat.


He needed something soft, fast-hardening, and heat-reactive.


Pyro jelly.


No time for second guesses. He lobbed another vial toward a shoulder vent. The sap splattered—bubbled—began to congeal in the opening.


The suit turned, the operator clearly underestimating him. It stomped toward the rex, ignoring him completely.


“Keep your distance,” Tetsuya barked. The rex obeyed, loping backward.


The raptors swarmed, snapping uselessly at the mech’s flanks.


He aimed for the lower vents. Lobbed another. The sap struck, boiling on contact, but thickening in the process. The suit’s heat was increasing.


Another puff of steam. Bigger this time.


The vents were ballooning with pressure.


He signaled the raptors: “Rear leg!”


They dove at the left leg as the rex spun around and delivered a kick to the right. The mech staggered. Caught itself with the claw—then buckled. The raptors hit the left leg hard. It crumpled fully, the rex catching its balance.


The pressure screamed now.


A vent cap burst.


He hurled his final sap orb—right into the breach. It sealed over like molten amber.  Heat waves visibly rose from the mech now.


Inside, the moss browned. Wilted.  How much time did he have?


Not much.  The operator finally reacted. The mech lurched forward—abandoning the rex—charging directly at Tetsuya.


He inhaled deep from his pipe.


Then ran straight at the thing.


The claw swiped at his head. The chainsaw dropped toward his legs.


He jumped. Over the blade. Hit the ground sliding—on his knees—under the claw, between the knees.


Rolled to the side.


“Now!” he barked.


The raptors launched in a wave, crashing down on the overheated chassis. It buckled further. They pushed away, adding further downward momentum, and the rex—free now—slammed down on top.  It began ripping at anything it could bite. 


Metal screamed.


Steam shrieked.


The suit’s frame bulged.


“Run!” he shouted.


The raptors scattered.


The rex turned to flee—


Too late.


The boiler exploded.




Aftermath


Down his rexes. Out of pyro jelly. No way to light his remaining paralytic orbs. Half his bolts had been on the trihorn—no room in his bag for all of them.


They found the alpha, gutted and smashed. Looked like the whole herd had walked over him. His bulk had taken out more of the lances than they’d probably expected—though several human corpses lay trampled nearby. No sign of the rest of the herd.  His extra supplies were trash now.


He followed what was now obviously a manmade path uphill. The ground was a chaotic paste of footprints. Spooked, the trihornadillos had stampeded. And hadn’t stopped for anything. The shattered outer gate was proof. Further up: thick metal doors, bowed inward. The wrong way.


Inside was what he could only call a facility.


It had originally been a cave. Now it was trashed. Completely. The herd had gone everywhere. He found corpses—sometimes trihornadillo, often human. All flattened. Smeared. Resembling toothpaste.


They’d chosen a base big enough for their mech to operate. That had been a mistake. The herd had panicked and surged through every corridor, every lab, every control station. Human resistance only fueled their terror.


He found them in what must have been the mech hangar. A half-constructed unit still hung in the corner—similar design to the one he’d just blown sky-high.


The trihorns flared their nostrils at him, but the scent of their fallen alpha still clung to his clothes and saddle. It seemed to calm them. One approached—bigger, scarred, with torn scale along the frill. He thought it might have been one of the males that had challenged him earlier.


He blew spores into its face.

You have tamed a creature for 5 hours.
Pack of followers gained: 12.


He didn't want his raptors clearing the corridors. He liked them. And these beasts could take an explosive bolt better.


Room by room, hallway by hallway, he pushed deeper. The herd did the heavy lifting—literally. Humans still alive put up uncoordinated resistance. He lost a few trihorns, but subdued more along the way. With each skirmish, the herd grew. Mostly they just ran the humans over, but when needed, he shot from behind. He was getting good at it. Even without his pipe, he could find the weak points.


Eventually he reached a corridor too narrow for the beasts.


He patted Momo-chan on the neck.


They were going in alone.

They followed the path to a single door.


He tried the handle. Locked.


You didn’t lock a door unless something important was behind it.


From his pocket, Tetsuya drew a pair of bone picks—filed thin and curved just right. He’d done a stint as a locksmith in his youth. Old hands found old rhythms. A little tension, a little feel…


Click.


He reached for the handle—but a warm shape shouldered in front of him.


Momo-chan.


She looked back at him, eyes intense, focused. It wasn’t a request.


He hesitated.


Then opened the door.


She surged forward.


The room exploded in chaos.


A dozen orderlies. Slingshots raised, explosives already chambered. Most were aiming for her.


The first volley flew wild—rushed and jittery. Only a few found their mark. One clipped her armor, another burst near her flank. Shrapnel shredded into her side.


She staggered. Snarled. Kept going.


Tetsuya shoved through the doorway behind her, already puffing smoke into his lungs, already breathing slow.


The fire washed his senses into clarity. Time slowed.


He saw her barrel into the center of them. She leapt—unstoppable, a blur of claws and fury—ripping into the first man she reached. His armor crumpled. His throat disappeared. She landed hard. But something was wrong.


A round had caught her mid-turn. She spun, faltered.


The third volley came. They had their target now. Her pale scales made her easy to see. They clustered their fire.


Five, six, seven explosions, tightly grouped.


The room rocked.


She collapsed in the smoke.


He screamed.


He fired.
Reloaded.
Fired again.


He didn’t aim. He didn’t think. Just acted.


Each bolt found a body. A head. A throat.


Men fell. Screaming. Twisting. Coughing blood and begging for mercy between spasms.


His raptors surged in behind him, fifteen strong. They screamed too—but not in pain. Rage. Pain-shaped rage.
They tore through the survivors like fire through dry grass.


But it didn’t matter.


Tetsuya dropped to his knees, scrambling to her side, bolts forgotten.


Momo-chan lay still, her blood pooling fast under her chest. Her head was down. One foreclaw still flexed. Her tail twitched once. Then nothing.


He touched her snout. Warm. Too warm.


Her pink eyes, always sharp, were losing their light.


She blinked once.


Then stopped.


His breath hitched. His fingers curled against her cheek. He whispered something, but no sound came.


The raptors formed a circle around them, quiet now. The killing done. The rage spent.


He wiped his face, unaware of the tears. They just mixed with blood.


His pipe lay on the floor, cracked.


He left it.


He expected the raptors to turn on him.  He reached for his spores.  One by one they knelt to him as they had for her.  The leader of the pack, the one who brought them meat.


It was time to see what was in this room.
