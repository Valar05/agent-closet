Still, standing around useless didn’t sit right with me. I tried to help where I could, handing tools to Vic or carrying parts for Jack, but mostly I just got in the way. I wasn’t exactly an engineer, and my attempts to "help" usually ended with someone glaring at me while muttering about amateurs.
Alexis wasn’t much better. She hovered near the edges of the repair crews, her face pale and her movements jittery. She hadn’t said much since Clyne. Couldn’t blame her. None of us really had.
________________


Sienna and Alexis caught my eye.
I found myself watching them more than I should’ve. The way Sienna talked to Alexis, her voice soft and steady, the way she kept her hand on the kid’s shoulder when they walked—it stirred something in me. Something I hadn’t felt in a long, long time.
Nova used to be like that. She had this way of calming people, of making everything feel manageable even when the world was falling apart. I hadn’t let myself think about her much since she died. Couldn’t. But now… watching Sienna and Alexis, it was like that part of me—the part that used to feel—was waking up again. And it hurt.
________________


Alexis’s parents were still back on Earth.
I knew their story. They’d been the ones to cover her escape to the railroad, pulling every string they could to get her out of the dome before someone turned her into a tool. But they hadn’t gotten away unscathed. Word was they were in a labor camp somewhere, paying the price for her freedom.
Sienna and I had stepped in, filling the roles Alexis’s parents couldn’t. Maybe older siblings, maybe stand-ins for something more. I didn’t let myself dwell on it too much. The implications were… well, something. Something complicated.
She didn’t talk about them, her parents. But when Sienna touched her shoulder or sat with her during meals, you could see a flicker of relief cross her face. Like maybe, just for a second, she felt safe.
________________


The Dome Held Its Breath
It wasn’t just the broken systems or the limping engines or the strained life support. It was the silence. The whole dome felt like it was waiting, holding its breath, waiting for something to give. Maybe it would be a part snapping loose in the engine room. Maybe it would be one more failure in life support. Maybe it would just be us, breaking under the weight of it all.
I tried to keep things light—cracked a few jokes about duct tape being the most important tool in interstellar travel. Got a few chuckles out of Vic, at least. But the truth was, I didn’t know if we’d make it to Mars. None of us did.
________________


But we kept moving.
Sienna worked herself into the ground keeping the crew alive. Alexis kept trying to help, even when she didn’t know how. And I… well, I didn’t do much of anything. But I watched them, and I thought about Nova, and I thought about Mars, and I thought about what the hell we’d find when we got there.
The implications of all of it—of the dome, of Alexis, of Clyne’s sacrifice, of Mars—it was all too much. So I did what I always did. I put my head down, and I kept going.
Sometimes, that’s all you can do.
