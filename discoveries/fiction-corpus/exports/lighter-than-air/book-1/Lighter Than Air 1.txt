Chapter 1: Lighter Than Air
At 14, life wasn’t great, but at least it was predictable. Every morning, I’d wake up in our cramped little apartment, choke down a ration bar (locust flour and sadness), and hop on my bike to school. New Dallas had been my home for seven years, and I’d gotten pretty good at navigating the dome-covered streets without getting mugged, run over, or eaten by a stray mutated insect.
If I’d known how the day was going to turn out, I might’ve stayed in bed. Or moved to Canada.
The dome wasn’t just there to keep the oversized bugs out (although they were part of the décor). It was our lifeline. Solar panels covered every inch, soaking up the light and keeping the city running. Of course, the bright patch of sunlight was reserved for the rich folks across town.
The rest of us? We got the gloom, the protein bars made from locusts, and the occasional boiled javelina on Christmas. If you squinted hard enough, you could almost pretend it was bacon.
Life wasn’t fancy, but it was livable. My dad and I had an apartment just big enough for the two of us and whatever canned goods we could scrounge. The TV only got two channels: news and reruns. I preferred the news because it had Potentiates—people who’d been gifted (or cursed, depending on your perspective) with powers after Albedo-8, a comet, dropped a glowing middle finger on Earth.
When I was seven, the comet streaked across the sky, leaving behind particles of Delta radiation that changed everything. The bugs were first. They mutated, grew, and turned the military into glorified exterminators. Then came the humans.
The first Potentiate was a little boy from West Africa who could grow to the size of a skyscraper. It was all over the news: “Superpowers are real!” screamed the headlines, and the world collectively lost its mind.  It made me long for the days when an ordinary person could squish a bug.
Meanwhile, I was watching cartoons and wondering if my parents would ever let me get a puppy. (Spoiler: They didn’t.)
The Cognitors were next—geniuses whose mutated brains saved the world from total collapse. They built domes, hydrogen cells, and wind turbines, turning us into an energy-efficient dystopia. It wasn’t great, but hey, at least we weren’t dead.
I was obsessed with Potentiates, especially the flashy ones. I used to dream about what it would be like to have powers. Then, one Tuesday morning, I found out.
It was a normal day, or as normal as things got in New Dallas. I was pedaling to school, trying to avoid the potholes that could swallow a bike whole, when I saw it: a hydrogen truck rumbling ahead of me.
Something didn’t feel right. The air was shimmering, like heat rising off asphalt in the summer. Except it wasn’t summer, and that wasn’t heat.
“Oh no,” I muttered.
The truck was leaking. A fine mist sprayed from the side, and before I could think, I was riding straight into it.
I flinched, expecting a face full of instant frostbite. Instead, the world… paused.
When I opened my eyes, I was surrounded by a floating bubble of liquid hydrogen. It just hung there, shimmering and humming, like I’d stumbled into a sci-fi movie. I didn’t know what was crazier—the fact that I wasn’t dead, or the fact that I could feel the hydrogen. Not touch it, but sense it, like an invisible thread connecting us.
I stepped back from my bike, and the bubble collapsed. The hydrogen splashed to the ground, hissing and evaporating. My bike, of course, froze solid, because fate has a sense of humor.
I stood there for a moment, my brain trying to catch up. Then I did what any rational person would do: I called my dad.


"Hey, Dad..."
“Hey, champ,” he answered, sounding way too chipper for the situation.
“Dad, there’s a hydrogen truck leaking all over Millway.”
“What?! Are you okay?”
“Yeah, but…” I hesitated. How do you tell your dad you might be a walking science experiment? “Something weird happened.  Something Super weird”
By the time I got home, Dad was pacing the living room like a caged tiger. He rushed over, checking me for frostbite, hypothermia, and probably alien implants.
“Are you hurt?” he asked for the millionth time.
“No, but, uh… I think I stopped the hydrogen.”
His face went pale. He sat down heavily on the couch, running a hand through his hair. “Your mom always worried this might happen. We were in a Delta hot zone when you were born. I guess…” He trailed off, looking at me like I’d sprouted a second head.
“I don’t want to leave,” I blurted. “They’ll send me away, won’t they?”
He didn’t answer, but his silence said enough.
It didn’t take long. By the afternoon, a black SUV pulled up outside. Two men in suits stepped out, their faces as cheerful as a funeral procession.
“Ruben Thommel?” the tall one asked, flashing a badge from the Department of Powers.
“That’s me,” I said, trying to sound brave and failing miserably.
He pulled out a tablet, swiping to a video of me surrounded by floating hydrogen. “You’re an Atomic. Class 1. Your abilities are rare and valuable to the government. Per Amendment 32, all Potentiates are required to serve in the United Powers Division until age 25.”
I barely heard the rest. My life was over.
The SUV ride to Camp Dominion was silent. The windows were so dark I couldn’t even see which direction we were going. All I could hear was the hum of the hydrogen engine, a quiet reminder of the life I was leaving behind.
When we finally stopped, I stepped out into the shadow of a massive concrete block. “Camp Dominion,” read the sign, with a motto beneath it: Power Demands Mastery. Cheery.
A woman with glowing glasses met me at the door, scanning me like I was a grocery item. “Ruben Thommel. Atomic, Class 1. Hydrogen. Welcome to Camp Dominion.”
Standing next to her was a boy about my age, hands stuffed in his pockets. “Victor,” she said. “He’ll show you around.”
Victor gave me a half-smile. “Follow me,” he said, leading me into the concrete labyrinth.
Camp Dominion was everything you’d expect from a government-run training facility: gray, sterile, and soul-sucking. No windows, no decorations, just endless halls of permacrete and the faint hum of electricity.
Victor led me to a room marked 417. “Home sweet home,” he said, pushing the door open.
The room was tiny: two beds, two desks, and a bathroom the size of a closet. I dropped my bag on the bed closest to the door, trying not to think about what I’d just signed up for.
Victor clapped me on the shoulder. “Rule number one: Work hard, or get crushed. This place doesn’t care if you’re tired, scared, or homesick. You want respect? Earn it.”
I nodded, swallowing the lump in my throat.
Inside, though, I made a promise: I wouldn’t just survive. I’d figure out my powers. I’d master them. And I’d make sure no one controlled my life ever again.
Big plans, less sleep. I lay awake, staring at the ceiling, listening to Vic’s soft, even breathing from the other bed. I tried to match his rhythm, focusing on my own. Breathe in. Hold. Breathe out, slower.
But my attention kept drifting to the vial of hydrogen on my desk. It was impossible to ignore, like a beacon. Or bacon. Either way, it drew me in.
I sat up, unable to resist the pull. The vial was clear to the eye, but to me, it buzzed with potential, swirling like a storm trapped in glass. I reached out with my mind, giving it a gentle nudge. The gas spun faster.
Could I...?
I grabbed the hydrogen more firmly with my thoughts, trying to recreate that instinctive control I’d felt on Millway Street. The gas froze—not in temperature, but in place.
Then, at my will, the vial lifted off the desk and floated toward me. Cool. Telekinesis. Kind of.
I caught it between my palms, rolling it experimentally. The “feel” of the hydrogen was odd—amorphous and squishy, like holding air that didn’t want to stay still. I pressed it down, compressing it. The resistance grew the harder I pushed, like trying to squeeze a balloon that didn’t want to pop.
On a whim, I twisted the cap off the vial. The gas hissed as it escaped, eager to bond with the oxygen in the room and form water. But I held it tight, isolating it with an iron grip. Without the weight of the glass, moving it was easier—almost natural.
Experimentally, I gathered the hydrogen into a tight sphere and hurled it at the wall. It struck with a soft pop, uncompressing in a puff of harmless vapor.
I tried to pull the scattered gas back, but it was already reacting with the air, dissipating before I could grab hold. I’d have to be faster next time.
A dull ache throbbed in my head from the effort. I let out a long breath and collapsed back onto the bed. Sleep claimed me before I even hit the pillow.


Chapter 2: Trial by Alarm Clock


The promised alarm came, and it was worse than I could’ve imagined. A siren blared through the room like it was trying to peel my brain out of my skull. I shot upright, tangled in my blanket, flailing like I was drowning.


Victor was already out of bed, brushing his teeth with the calm resignation of someone who’d survived this hell for years. He glanced at me, foam dribbling from his mouth, and smirked. “Fun, huh? It’s like they designed it to make you beg for the sweet release of death.”
I groaned, dragging myself upright. “You get used to this?”
“Eventually,” he said, rinsing his mouth. “But don’t worry, by the time it stops bothering you, you’ll have much bigger things to hate.”
The Inspection Gauntlet
We barely had time to throw on uniforms and flatten our beds before the senior students arrived for inspection. They prowled the line like sharks, looking for the slightest hint of weakness.
One senior—a wiry guy with eyes that practically glowed with self-righteousness—stopped at my bed. He leaned in so close I could smell the peppermint gum he was chewing. “Hmm,” he murmured, running a finger along the edge of the blanket. When he didn’t find anything wrong, his face twisted in disappointment, like I’d personally ruined his morning.
Victor, standing beside me, muttered under his breath, “Better luck next time, champ.”
Breakfast with a Side of Disappointment


The mess hall was packed with students shoveling food into their mouths like it was a race. My breakfast was a bowl of oatmeal speckled with candied locusts. The crunch was… unsettling.
Victor wolfed his down without a second thought. “Not bad today,” he said, licking his spoon. “The sugar-to-bug ratio is decent.”
I stared at my bowl, poking it with the spoon. “This is what counts as decent?”
“Lower your expectations,” Victor said with a grin. “And then lower them again. That’s the Dominion way.”
Enter the Sparring Ring
After breakfast, we headed to the sparring hall. The room was massive, filled with rings surrounded by cheering students. My name was at the very bottom of the Copper leaderboard, practically buried beneath the weight of everyone above me.
Victor clapped me on the back. “You’re up first. Time to make a name for yourself, bubble boy.
I climbed into the ring, my legs shaking. My first opponent was Dennis Park, a wiry guy with a smirk that could curdle milk.
“Dennis Park,” he announced, bouncing on his toes. “Fastest kid in Copper. You’re about to become a cautionary tale.”
The bell rang.  I noticed a hiss of gas and my hydrogen sense exploded.  They were supplementing me!  It wasn't enough.
Dennis was fast—blindingly fast. He darted forward, his fist slamming into my face before I even had time to react. I hit the mat hard, my nose exploding in pain.
Dennis laughed, strutting across the ring. “Too easy! Come on, newbie—give me something to work with!”
For a second, I thought about staying down. But then I saw his smirk, saw the way the crowd was eating it up. Something in me snapped.
The Hydrogen Trick
I focused on his breathing, the faint rhythm of air moving in and out. Hydrogen. I could feel it, part of the air around him, part of every breath he took.
I gathered it, tightening it around him like an invisible net. Dennis faltered, coughing.
“What the hell?” he squeaked, his voice high-pitched.
His movements slowed as the air thinned. The crowd started laughing, jeering at him. “What’s wrong, Dennis? Seems like you've got plenty of gas to go around!”
Dennis glared at them, his steps clumsy now, his punches weak. He tried one last desperate charge, but his legs gave out, and he crumpled to his knees.
The bell rang.
“Winner: Ruben Thommel,” the instructor announced.
Victor appeared at my side, grinning. “Not bad for your first time, bubble boy. You’ve got a talent for making people look stupid.”
Enter Sienna
Before I could process what had just happened, a girl stepped out from the crowd. Her sharp gray eyes locked onto mine, and she strode toward me with a glowing vial in hand.
“For the pain,” she said, holding it out.
I hesitated, then drank it in one gulp. It tasted like bitter grass, but the throbbing in my nose faded almost immediately.
“Don’t thank me yet,” she said, grabbing my face with cold hands. Her fingers pressed against my jaw and temple, tilting my head back.


“What are you—”


Pop!


My nose snapped back into place, and I yelped.  The rush of cold that washed over me was all-consuming.  In its wake was an invigorating energy, like I'd just taken an arctic bath to wake up.
“There,” she said, stepping back. “Good as new.”
I blinked, touching my nose gingerly. “You could’ve warned me.”
Her lips quirked into a faint smile. “And miss that reaction? No chance.”
The Tyson Fight
My next opponent was Tyson Hale, a walking mountain with rock-covered skin. He stood at the edge of the ring, rolling his shoulders, his face calm and unreadable. Then he did something I hadn’t expected.
He closed his eyes and began taking slow, measured breaths, in and out. Sharp inhales, deep exhales, followed by a long pause. It reminded me of divers prepping to hold their breath.
The crowd buzzed with anticipation. I realized Tyson had been watching my match with Dennis. He knew I could mess with breathing.
The bell rang, and Tyson closed the distance with terrifying inevitability. I hurled a compressed hydrogen sphere at his knee, praying for a miracle.
The sphere hit with a soft pop and did nothing. Tyson didn’t even blink.
“Best you got?” he drawled, his voice slow and mocking.
He lunged, faster than I expected. His stone-covered fist slammed into my side, and I flew across the ring, landing hard. Pain lanced through my ribs, and the air rushed out of my lungs.
I scrambled to my feet, wobbling as I tried to focus. I could feel the hydrogen in the air, but Tyson wasn’t giving me time to think. He came at me again, his movements deliberate, almost casual.
Desperation took over. I pulled the hydrogen around me into a dense shield, hoping it would hold.
It didn’t.
Tyson smashed through it like it was paper, grabbing me by the shoulders and lifting me off the ground. “Game over,” he said, before hurling me into the wall.
The impact knocked the wind out of me. I crumpled to the floor, gasping.
The bell rang.
“Winner: Tyson Hale,” the instructor announced.
As I sat there, clutching my ribs, Sienna knelt beside me again. Her hands were cold but steady as she pressed them against my chest. The soothing coldness spread through me, and the pain began to fade.
“Better?” she asked, her voice softer now.
“Yeah,” I said, breathing easier. “Thanks.”
She stood, brushing off her hands. “You fought smart. But Tyson isn’t someone you can outthink.”
“No kidding,” I muttered, wincing as I sat up.
She gave me a small, almost encouraging smile. “You’ll learn.”
As she walked away, Victor crouched beside me, smirking. “Welcome to Dominion, bubble boy. Where every victory makes you a target, and every loss reminds you just how much you suck.”
I groaned, already dreading tomorrow.
Lunch Date
Lunch was a strange mix of foods. The main dish was a grilled protein patty — likely made from crickets — topped with a thick, peppery sauce. On the side were roasted vegetables that looked normal enough, but they had a metallic aftertaste that made me wonder where they’d been grown. At least the bread roll was soft and warm, if not perfectly bread like.


I grabbed my tray and scanned the room, looking for Vic. Someone nearby mentioned that the somel matches were still going. With nothing better to do, I glanced around and spotted Sienna sitting alone at a table. Her dark braid hung over one shoulder, and she poked at her food with her fork, her gaze distant.


I hesitated for a moment, then made my way over.


“Mind if I join you?” I asked, setting my tray down carefully.


She looked up, her sharp gray eyes studying me for a moment before she shrugged. “Only if you like being an outcast.”


I sat down across from her. “I’m new, so my reputation is fluid.”


That earned me a faint smirk. “Fluid.  Like hydrogen.”


I took a bite of my protein patty, chewing slowly as I watched her pick at her vegetables. “So… why are you an outcast? You don’t seem like the type to get in trouble.”


She stabbed a piece of squash with her fork, spinning it absently. “Because I don’t play the game.”


“What game?”


“The healer game.” She leaned back, crossing her arms. “You’ve seen them, right? The other healers? They walk around like they own the place.”


I nodded. I hadn't been here long, but I'd seen them.


“They’re not just here to heal,” she said. “They’re here to control.”


I frowned. “What do you mean?”


“They decide who gets healed and who doesn’t. And if you get hurt, they have discretion over how much they heal.”


“Discretion?”


“They’ll fix the big stuff — broken bones, internal bleeding. But they can leave nerve damage. Or weakened joints. Or pain that never really goes away.”


I stared at her, stunned. “They can do that?”


“They do it all the time. Officially, it’s to teach a lesson. If you take stupid risks, you’re supposed to feel the consequences. But really, it’s about power. It’s about reminding people who’s in charge.”


I pushed my tray away, my appetite suddenly gone. “That’s… messed up.”


“It is.” She poked at her food again. “If you kiss the right asses, you get full rec. No lingering pain. But if you fall out of favor… you suffer.”


“And you don’t play along?”


“No.” Her voice was firm. “I believe healing should be complete. If someone’s hurt, it’s my job to fix them. Not to teach lessons or play politics.”


“And they don’t like that.”


“They hate it.” She smirked, though there was no humor in it. “I make them look bad.”


I sat back, thinking it over. The idea that healers could decide to leave someone in pain on purpose made me uneasy. “Has that happened to anyone?”


Her expression darkened. “Yeah. His name was Nathan Coyle.”


I watched her carefully. “What happened to him?”


“Nathan was reckless. He pissed off Rachel one day.  He was always pushing the bounds, always trying to prove he belonged. One day, he dislocated his shoulder. Badly. I wanted to heal him completely, but the other healers overruled me.”


She set her fork down, her hand trembling slightly. “They repaired the shoulder just enough for him to keep fighting. But they left the nerve damage. He couldn’t lift his arm without pain.”


I swallowed hard. “And he’s still here?”


She shook her head. “Not really. He’s… broken. He doesn’t talk much anymore. Just stares at the wall, mumbling to himself. They call it a lesson. I call it cruelty.” She sighed.  “I tried to help him later.  But it was too late for my powers.  And there were… consequences.” She didn't elaborate.


The image of Nathan sitting alone, trapped in his own mind, made my stomach turn. “Why would they do that?”


“Because they could.” Her gaze locked on mine, her eyes filled with quiet fury. “They said it would teach him discipline. But it just broke him.”


We sat in silence for a moment. The low hum of the mess hall filled the space between us, but I barely heard it.


“You’re better than them,” I said quietly.


She blinked. “What?”


“You’re better than them,” I repeated. “You’re a good person, and they aren't.” I said with finality.


She stared at me for a long moment, her expression unreadable. Then, slowly, her lips curved into a small, genuine smile.


“Thanks, Ruben.”


I smiled back. “Anytime.”
Resuming her food with more enthusiasm, she said, “So you've heard my story, what's yours?”


I shrugged and told her.  “The usual.  Hot zone in utero.  Split parents.  My dad trying to be a scientist when toddlers design fusion drives.”


She nodded, chewing thoughtfully. “And now you're here. Based on your fight with Dennis, I think you'll do well with some training. Just don't get too comfortable. This place… it changes people.”


After her story, I was beginning to see what she meant.


I nodded in agreement, and we sat in companionable silence for a few minutes, the noise of the mess hall fading into the background.


She showed me to my next class in the bewildering complex, “They want you confused, off balance.  Makes it easier to mold you.”
I thanked her and walked into class.
Instruction
I stepped into the classroom. It was pretty small, actually. A few students were arrayed at lab tables, each station equipped with sleek, modern equipment that looked far more advanced than anything I'd ever seen. The teacher stood at the front, near a podium and a high-tech smartboard that flickered to life as we entered.


The man was tall and lean, with glasses that reflected the overhead lights, making it hard to see his eyes. He gave me a nod and clasped his hands.


“Well, it looks like we're all here. We are all gaseous Atomics, and today we will be learning how to compress our respective elements into a liquid.”


He gave me a pointed look.  “As you might know, many of the gasses we use are in short supply in the regular air of the world.  To help you acclimate to your powers, we supplement the air with nontoxic elements, such as hydrogen and helium.  More is provided during your sparring and in training rooms.”


His voice was calm but carried an edge of authority that made it clear he expected results.


He held up a vial filled with a faint cloud of vapor. “However we will not be covering pulling your element from the air today.  You will find some in one of these, already isolated. This will make the process easier—but it will not be easy by any means.”


He glanced down at his own vial, and I watched in fascination as the cloud of vapor swirled before condensing into a small sphere of liquid. A moment later, it dissipated back into gas.


“You will find that brute force will not do the trick,” he continued. “You must master the ability to alter temperature, to slow the movements of the molecules so they will bond. But you must also compress them. Too much force, and they will scatter. Too little, and they will remain as they are.”


The room was utterly silent as he spoke, the tension thick with expectation.


“This skill is essential for those of you who wish to survive in combat. Compression is the foundation of many techniques. Learn it well, or fall behind.”


He continued the lecture for a while, breaking down the science behind molecular bonds and the balance required to maintain pressure and temperature. It was fascinating—but also overwhelming. When he finally cut us loose to practice, I felt my heart hammering in my chest.


I glanced down at the vial of hydrogen in front of me. It was completely clear, but my senses told me it was pure, slightly compressed by the containment. Around me, the other students were examining their own vials. The flashiest one belonged to a girl handling neon, her vial glowing faintly with an otherworldly red light.


I took a deep breath and focused on my task. I'd had some success compressing hydrogen before, but I'd never tried to alter its temperature. I decided to start there.


I held the vial in my hands, feeling its cool surface. The room was slightly chilly already, and I could feel a faint vibration of energy from the hydrogen inside. I tried to pull the energy out, imagining it leaving the molecules.


Nothing happened.


I frowned, closing my eyes. Stillness. That was the key. I pictured the molecules slowing down, their frantic bouncing easing into a calm, peaceful state.


Stillness. Peace. Cold.


Slowly, I began to feel a difference. The energy inside the vial seemed to lessen, and when I opened my eyes, I saw that the glass had frosted over. I'd done it. Well, kind of.


A surge of excitement ran through me, but I forced myself to stay calm. Temperature was only half the battle. Now I had to compress it.


I imagined the hydrogen atoms drawing closer together, pressing inward without scattering. I guided them gently, balancing pressure and temperature. It felt like holding my breath and balancing on the edge of a knife at the same time.


But every time I got close, the gas slipped free, returning to its original state.


I wasn’t the only one struggling. The girl with the neon vial let out a low growl, her frustration mounting. She gripped the vial like she was about to hurl it across the room.


I was close, though. I could feel it.


As the class neared its end, I took a final breath and focused everything I had on the hydrogen. Stillness. Pressure. Balance.


Suddenly, I saw it—a tiny bead of liquid hydrogen hovered in the center of the vial. It was perfectly clear, shimmering faintly before evaporating into mist. It only lasted a second, but I'd done it.


“You're a quick study.”


The voice came from right next to me, making me jump. I nearly dropped my vial.


Gault stood there, watching me with an expression that might have been approval—or something colder. His glasses caught the light again, hiding his eyes.


“Hydrogen may be the easiest liquid to form, but it’s rare to see someone do it on day one.” He leaned in slightly, lowering his voice. “I’ve got my eye on you, Thommel.”


I swallowed, unsure how to respond. “Thank you, sir.”


Gault gave a curt nod and moved on to the next student.


The class ended shortly after, and I packed up my things, slipping the vial back into its protective case. My hands still trembled from the effort, and a dull ache had settled in my chest.


As I left the room, I was surprised to see Vic waiting for me in the hall.


He grinned as I approached.
“Not bad for a first day,” he said. “Gault doesn’t usually notice people unless they really screw up—or really impress him.”


I raised an eyebrow. “And which one do you think I did?”


Vic’s grin widened. “I guess we’ll find out.”


By 2 PM, I was already ready for bed, but instead, I found myself trudging into Physical Education and Combat Training—a fluorescent-lit hellhole that reeked of sweat, disinfectant, and despair. Sergeant Rourke, a human brick wall in a sleeveless uniform, barked us into line, informing us with grim enthusiasm that our powers wouldn’t save us in close combat. 


He wasn’t kidding. The warm-up alone felt like a death march disguised as calisthenics, and by the time we moved on to takedowns, Vic had slammed me into the mat so many times I was pretty sure my spine was filing for divorce. Sparring was worse—I got flattened by a guy built like a vending machine, my ribs screaming in protest while Rourke barked about pain being our new best friend. 


By the end, Vic handed me a towel with a pitying look and a cheerful, “Just try not to die, okay?” Great advice, really. Tomorrow couldn’t be worse… probably.


Dinner
By the time I dragged myself into the next classroom—a standard math and science combo, according to the schedule—I was almost too exhausted to function. The fatigue from combat training still gnawed at my muscles, and the headache from Gault’s compression lesson kept threatening to flare up behind my eyes. To my surprise, the classroom looked almost normal: rows of desks, a bored-looking instructor, a digital board humming with formulas. We were supposed to review basic algebraic expressions used in energy rationing models—things like figuring out how many kilojoules a domed city could generate from solar and hydrogen sources, and how to allocate them efficiently. It was strangely ordinary and terrifyingly important all at once.


Half the class listened intently, pencils scratching as the teacher explained rate-of-consumption graphs, but the other half seemed to be dozing or daydreaming. I tried my best to focus, though my gaze kept drifting to the clock, counting down until we’d be released for dinner. The teacher, Ms. Raine, assigned a slew of equations to solve by tomorrow—something about balancing city-wide humidity extraction with daily usage. Vic would find it hilarious, no doubt, but I just felt dread piling on top of my weariness.


When the final bell rang, I shuffled to the mess hall with my bag slung over one shoulder, wishing fervently for a bed and a full night’s sleep. The dinner line was shorter than usual, and I ended up with a tray of rehydrated stew, some grayish vegetable medley, and a chunk of that pseudo-bread they’ve been trying to pass off as real. I looked around, spotting Vic first—lounging in a corner table, flipping his favorite coin between his fingers. Sienna was nowhere in sight, and I was debating whether to search for her or just collapse in a seat when I spotted her stepping into line, her dark braid swishing against her back. Her expression was distant, maybe a little pensive, but she perked up when she saw me waving.


We convened at Vic’s table, and for a second he seemed taken aback by Sienna’s presence. She set her tray down carefully, eyeing the lumpy stew with obvious skepticism. Vic raised an eyebrow and offered a lopsided grin. “Didn’t expect to see you here,” he said, pausing in his coin tricks. “You’re usually off studying or... healing people.”


Sienna shrugged, sitting down opposite him. “I’m allowed to eat, aren’t I?” Her tone wasn’t harsh, but it carried a trace of well-worn defiance. Then she glanced at me with a faint smile. “Besides, Ruben’s about the only person who doesn’t mind my company these days.”


Vic chuckled, pushing his tray forward. “Well, I guess he’s a better judge of character than me. Name’s Vic, by the way.” He held out a hand, and Sienna hesitated just a moment before shaking it. “Sienna,” she said, giving him a quick, polite nod.


An awkward pause settled in, broken only by the clink of utensils and the murmur of other students around us. Finally, Vic gestured at my tray. “How was homework, bubble boy? Didn’t see you in the dorm earlier.”


I shrugged. “You know, trying not to fail basic math. Ms. Raine’s dumping a ton of equations on us.” I glanced at Sienna. “What about you? Any chance the healer classes are friendlier than Gault’s or Rourke’s?”


Her mouth quirked in a wry smile. “They’re not too bad, except for the politics. It’s all about who you heal, how fully you heal them, whether you’re following the rules. I’d rather just patch people up without leaving them half-crippled, but that’s... frowned upon, apparently.”


Vic let out a low whistle. “Yikes. Guess the healer clique’s as intense as the rest of this place.” He chewed a piece of bread, grimacing at the texture. “And here I thought we’d get a decent meal for dinner.”


Sienna tapped her spoon against the edge of the stew bowl. “You get used to it, or so they tell me.” She gave me a look that said she doubted that very much. I offered a small smile, which she returned.


We ate in relative silence for a while, none of us quite sure how to fill the new space between us.
