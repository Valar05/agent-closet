It started with a plague of locusts. Some people said it was the end times.


Dylan called it Tuesday.


Jumpy’s mandible clicked like a metronome. Sounding out danger. The dog-sized jumping spider was utterly silent otherwise.


He became aware of the buzzing next. Jumpy clicked faster, like a Geiger counter climbing.


Dylan dropped his spear. Grabbed the bottle. Vodka burned his mouth. The lighter clicked.


The flies exploded from the body, leaving only stripped bone. He blew into the cloud. Jumpy waited, clicks slowing.


The flies broke and fled the meal. Jumpy tackled the bones. Marrow snapped. Dylan crunched the flies. Not bad this way.


The crunching stopped. Rapid clicks.


Dylan snatched his spear and dropped low. The scorpion exploded from the ground.


Jumpy didn’t hesitate, leaping. Pincers snapped as the tail arced overhead.


Jumpy sprang back as the tail struck, the creature nearly skewering itself. It staggered as the spider venom took hold.


The venom didn’t take it down. It made an opening.


He hurled the spear at its face. It struck true. A dying throe snapped the spear in two.


Good trade. A stick for a week’s food.


Then he heard the humming. Jumpy clicked, belatedly.


No time to grab the spear. He ran.


They threw themselves into the scorpion hole.


Light blotted out above. Darkness below. The hum was all.


Then—light. Luminescent fungus clung to the walls.


The tunnel widened.


The hum continued, muted. They waited where the tunnel opened into a wider chamber. Water dripped.


Dylan patted Jumpy. His bristles were still standing on end.


Jumpy leaned into the touch. His spinnerets clacked. Then he froze.


Dylan moved on instinct. Something skittered on stone behind him.


A baby scorpion. Smaller. Brighter.


Jumpy skittered back, clicking a warning. Dylan’s hand closed on a rock as he fell. He threw.


Smush.


More venom than its mother. Much more fragile.


The tail was intact. He took it from behind, careful. Awkward as a weapon. Deadly anyway.


Jumpy did not settle. The hum was thinning. Something else replaced it.


Shuttling. Thousands of legs.


Dylan turned. “Web.”


Jumpy sprayed the tunnel. The first babies stuck fast. The rest climbed over them.


The sounds behind them faded. The sounds ahead grew louder.


They burst into sunlight.


The mother was gone. The spear haft was gone. Everything living was stripped to the roots.


The spearhead remained. Steel wasn’t food.




Dylan noticed one more thing the mandibles hadn’t touched. Cans.  Near the body.
Their labels were stripped, but the metal was proof against locusts. It didn’t taste like food.


He scooped them up without slowing.
The scorpions didn’t like the light. But they might be hungry. He didn’t wait to see which instinct won.
The land was devastated around them.  A wide swath that had been dry but populated with hardy plants was now barren.
One thing Jumpy couldn’t do was find water.
He tipped the last drops from his wsterskin onto his lips.
There was water in the cave. Not worth what lived with it.


Dylan’s hand brushed his bag. The leather was cracking.
It needed fat, his grandfather would have said.
Dylan’s world was lean protein, as his grandmother would put it.
 He pulled out an unmarked can.  Punctured it.  Dog food.  He shared with Jumpy.  As in all things.


As they crossed the waste, Dylan heard the hum. Different. Thinner. A dying hum.


He needed water. He went toward the sound anyway.


That was when he saw the flower.
As tall as him. Tattered. Half eaten. Alive.
Grass. Grass meant water.
He tipped the flowers head to himself and sipped the water that collected there.  Floral and feminine.


The hum rose, but it had lost its weight. More whine than throb.


He found the first bee. No stinger. Dead.
A bumblebee. Big as a car in the old world.


Beyond it, the locust.
It traced broken patterns in the dirt, dragging its head in circles.
A stinger the size of a harpoon still pulsed venom into it.


Dylan watched the pattern.


Then he drew the baby scorpion’s stinger.
He drove it into a joint.


The locust twitched. Then stilled.


He pulled out the spearhead and pierced the leg.
It left him with a long, serrated strip of chitin.


He scraped away the spikes to make a handhold.
A short spear. Strong.
He wrapped the head with twine, tying a familiar knot.


Jumpy hadn’t waited. He was already into the bee.
Dylan cut into the abdomen, well clear of the spider's bite.


It wasn’t water, but the meat was juicy.
He wrapped some in a leaf from the flower and ate.
Sweet. Crisp.
He thought of apple blossoms. He could taste them in the flesh.
Trees needed water.


He waited for Jumpy to finish.
The spider waddled now, swollen with juice.
He would want sleep soon.


So did Dylan.
They moved some distance from the bodies and found a patch of soft soil.
They dug down—Dylan with the collapsible spade, Jumpy with a flurry of legs.
Sticks and leaves, webbed together, formed a trap door over the entrance.
They curled up.
Darkness fell.
CHAPTER 2
The bee was gone in the morning, and the locust lay ringed in dead rats. Some were the size of Jumpy. They gave the bodies a wide berth.  Scorpion venom did not sleep.


The hive called, for some reason. He returned to the flower. Dew had collected in its bowl. He drank. The stream versus honey. He chose honey.


There was none.


The hive was stripped to paper. Then he saw it—a wall of bees. Dead ones. They had stung each other, poisoning their own flesh.


“Hello?” a small voice called. “I can hear you out there, two-feet.”


Dylan hesitated. “The locusts are gone.”


The pile of bees wriggled. A girl emerged, clad in wax and crystallized honey. Dylan raised a hand. Jumpy froze.


She looked back at the bees, eyes heavy. “They were my friends.”


Dylan nodded. “They saved you.”




She bent down and patted Jumpy. “He’s cute,” she said softly.


Jumpy leaned into the touch, spinnerets clicking.


“I guess he likes you.”


Dylan trusted the spider’s instincts more than his own when it came to people.


He'd learned that lesson too well.
