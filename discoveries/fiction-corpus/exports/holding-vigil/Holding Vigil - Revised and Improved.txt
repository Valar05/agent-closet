Holding Vigil
THE CAGE WITH GLASS WALLS
INT. BOARDROOM – PARADISE PENITENTIARY COMPLEX
Glass walls. Steel floor. Clinical air that smells like nothing.
Lexen Vigil stands barefoot in the corner, shirt rolled to his elbows.
His knuckles are scarred. So are his eyes.
A heavy bag hangs beside him, its seams split, sand bleeding out like it tried to fight back.
The Paradise executives sit at a long table inside the room.
Suits. Hollow eyes. Fingers tapping at shimmering feeds.
Everything is artificial—except him.
Phones buzz.
Holograms flicker..
One man, the chairman, clears his throat.
He doesn’t look up when he speaks.
"We’re here to discuss the destruction of property. The orbital facility designated Paradise Vault. The deaths of two hundred staff. The loss of critical AI frameworks and—"
Thud.
Lexen hits the bag.
A slow, deliberate strike. Elbow deep.
The chain creaks. Sand shifts. The bag swings once.
Every feed in the room flickers.
Another exec tries to rush the script.
"You stole children—"
Lexen speaks without looking at them.
"I saved them. From you. From your simulations. From your perfect little dreams."
He turns, now facing the glass wall.
"You bred them in orbit. Grew them like roots. Pumped their minds full of sweetness before they ever touched dirt."
"That’s not the point—"
He takes another step.
"The point is you forgot what pain was. What hunger meant. What it costs to be real."
Another punch.
The bag swings.
"You made a heaven where no one had to try."
Thud.
"But when I burned it, they screamed not from pain—"
Thud.
"—but from waking up."
He lets the bag swing. Steps back.
Then, softer:
"You think I’m trapped in here."
Silence.
"You’re wrong."
"You’re in the cage with me."
"And you tapped out before the fight even started."
The bag swings in silence.
One final creak.
And the lights go out.
Every feed dies.
Every phone goes dark.
One by one, the holograms vanish.
Breath catches in the dark.
Then—glass breaks.

ORIGIN I – “THE QUIET BEFORE” (SCENE)
INT. VIGIL FAMILY DINING ROOM – EVENING
The room is spotless.
Not just clean—kept.
 Wooden table, well-oiled. Matching plates. Steam rising from a casserole. The clock on the wall ticks loud in the quiet.
5:03 PM.
Lexen’s father walks through the door, tie loosened just one notch. He kisses his wife’s cheek. Pulls out his chair. Sits.
Lexen, seventeen, sits across from his younger brother, hands folded. He’s already tall, already carrying himself like he’s somewhere else.
His mother serves. Her hands move with practiced ease.
“Heavenly Father,” his father begins, head bowed, voice low and certain, “we thank you for this meal, for this home, and for the love that binds us. Amen.”
They echo it.
Lexen says it last. Quietest.
They eat in silence for a moment. Silverware clinks. No phones. No screens.
His mother smiles.
“How was school?”
His brother launches into something about chemistry class. Lexen half-listens.
He glances at the wall behind her. A framed photo of Jesus with a lamb.
“Why would God let a kid starve while I’m sitting here eating shepherd’s pie?”
The room halts. Forks freeze.
His mother blinks. His brother stares.
His father wipes his mouth with a napkin. Calm.
“We’re blessed, son. That’s not something to fix. It’s something to be grateful for.”
Lexen leans back.
“Doesn’t feel blessed. Feels empty.”
“Empty of what?” his mom asks.  “You lack for nothing.”
“Something real.”

Later. Quiet.
Lexen sits alone in the dark, staring at the muted flicker of a TV that isn’t even on. His brother’s asleep. He walks past his parents door, which is cracked.
No performative prayers, these.  They're spoken in grief.  In desperation.
“Please, bring him back to you.  Let him love you as we do.”

EXT. MILITARY RECRUITMENT OFFICE – MONTHS LATER – DAY
He signs the papers with a hard pen grip. The recruiter grins.
“Looking for a career?”
Lexen shrugs.
“Looking for weight.”
Boot to the Face
He stepped off the transport like he didn’t know how to carry his own weight.
Too straight. Too clean. Hands clenched too tight at his sides, like posture could protect him.


The sun hit like a hammer. The tarmac shimmered. Boot camp already smelled like gun oil, piss, and desperation.
He wore it badly.


The others barked and shouted like it was theater. Lexen said nothing. Eyes forward. Heart... steady, but untethered. Like a dog waiting for someone to tell it what to bite.


A sergeant screamed inches from his ear.
He didn’t flinch.


That earned him attention. Not the good kind.
“You think you’re made of steel, recruit?”
He didn’t answer.
“We got a stoic! Got yourself a poet, boys!”
The squad laughed.
He didn’t.
He wasn’t trying to be quiet. He just had nothing worth saying.


The barracks were rows of misery—socks too short, beds too stiff, voices too loud.
Lexen unpacked what little he had: three shirts, one photo he didn’t look at, a clean field notebook with nothing inside.
He slept flat on his back the first night, eyes open.
The man beside him cried.
Lexen turned toward the wall.
Didn’t close his eyes.
In the morning, the sun hadn’t risen yet, but the yelling had.
He was already dressed. Already standing.


He didn’t know why.


He just knew he wanted to feel something. Anything. Even pain.
Boot camp didn’t break him.
It lit him.


He burned quiet at first. Just a flicker.
But every day added fuel: the sweat, the noise, the friction of skin against dirt.
He didn't resist it. He invited it.


He learned the rhythm of bruises. The weight of breath at the edge of collapse.


They yelled in his face. He didn't blink.


They made him first through the door. Again and again.
Paint rounds in his ribs, in his throat.
He started grinning after the hits.


The fire caught in his fists.
In his lungs.
In the ache he finally knew how to feed.


He met men and women who bled for each other without hesitation.
Who screamed through the last rep.
Who fought like wolves and laughed like children.


They weren't holy. They weren’t heroes.
They were alive. And Lexen fit there—in the blood and the strain and the breath that came in gasps.
For the first time, he felt earned exhaustion.
Not comfort.
Not numbness.
Purpose.
________________


Day three: room clearing drills. Fast. Loud. Full of paint rounds.


First man through always caught it worst.
Lexen took one in the neck—close range. A recruit panicked.


He dropped, choking. Not because it hurt, but because he hadn’t expected it.


That was the lesson:
Don’t expect. Don’t guess.
Move like you’ve already been shot.


Later that week, a killhouse run.


All foam weapons but one—faulty dummy with a real blade slipped into the grip.
No one noticed until Lexen breached the room.


He swept left, closed to subdue. The blade caught him on the thigh.
Clean. Sharp.


He dropped the dummy. Sat down. Didn’t scream.
The instructor cursed and called for a medtech.


The guy showed up with gauze and a bad attitude.
“Hold still.”
Lexen didn’t move.


“You’re either crazy or already cooked,” the tech muttered, winding the bandage.


Lexen didn’t answer. Flexed his leg twice.
Stood up.
Limped back to the line.


That night, most of the others went out—chasing noise, girls, drinks, whatever let them forget how close they'd come to breaking.  
Lexen stayed.


He sat on the back loading dock, leg wrapped in duct tape and training cloth, staring up like the stars owed him an answer.


A man dropped onto the bench beside him.  
Broad-shouldered. Crooked nose. Big hands.  
Didn’t speak.
Just offered him a flask.


Lexen stared at it.


The man finally said, “They send me here when the fresh ones start cracking.”  
A pause.  
“You didn’t crack.”


Lexen didn’t answer.


“It’s not poison,” the man added.


“I've heard otherwise.”


Regardless, Lexen took the flask.  
Uncapped it. Sniffed.  
Burnt sugar and smoke.


“It’ll quiet the fire,” the man said, smiling like he’d been where Lexen was now.


Lexen drank.


It went down hot—burned through his chest, carved a path to his stomach like liquid brass.


He coughed.  
Felt the blood rush in his ears.


And for the first time in months—maybe ever—he felt present.


Not drifting.  
Not waiting.  
Here.


He passed the flask back.  
The man didn’t take it.


“Yours now.”


Lexen looked down at it, then forward.  
The concrete. The stars.  
The slow throb in his leg.


He drank again.


Smokes
The yard was still steaming after drills.
Sun baking the asphalt. Gear clattered as squads stripped down and shook off the heat.


Lexen sat on the concrete barrier near the latrine wall—shirt off, hands wrapped from rope work, shoulders raw.
He didn’t talk. Didn’t wipe the sweat.


Just stared across the gravel like he was waiting for something that wouldn’t show.
A shadow cut across the sun.


“You earned that silence?” a voice asked.
Lexen looked up.


Sergeant Dorran.
One of the old-school bastards.
Flat nose. Burn scar down his neck.
Never raised his voice, but people moved when he blinked.


Lexen didn’t answer.
Dorran nodded like that was good enough.


He pulled a crumpled soft-pack from his breast pocket. Slapped one loose with the back of his hand.
Held it out.
Lexen hesitated.


“It ain’t a prize,” Dorran said. “It’s just what’s left when the day’s still chewing.”


Lexen took the cigarette. Tucked it between his lips.
Dorran lit it with a dented striker that smelled faintly of oil and boot polish.


Lexen inhaled.
Coughed once.


Dorran didn’t laugh.
 “You’ll get used to it.”
Lexen nodded. Eyes on the horizon.


“Not planning to.”
“You will anyway.”
Dorran walked off without waiting for more.


Lexen sat there until the cigarette burned down to the filter.
Didn’t smoke it fast.
Didn’t think about what it meant.


He just watched the smoke rise.
And felt something settle in his bones.
Graduation
The ceremony was held on the dry field behind the barracks.
No banners.
No bands.
Just folding chairs sinking into cracked dirt and a podium that looked like it had been stolen from a courthouse.
The brass said a few words.
Something about pride.
Something about duty.
Lexen stood in formation with the rest of his class, boots lined up edge to edge, sweat pooling at the small of his back.
He didn’t smile.
Didn’t fidget.
Just stared straight ahead, heart beating steady and slow.
They called names.
One by one, the recruits stepped forward. Took their papers. Shook hands with men who’d forget them by nightfall.
When they called Vigil, he moved without hesitation.
Took the envelope.
Nodded once.
Turned back to the line.
No salute.
No extra glance.
The instructors didn’t try to stop him.
They knew better.

By noon, they were gone.
The field cleared like it had never mattered.
Some packed for new assignments with grins split across their faces.
Some called their families.
Some got drunk before sundown.
Lexen just packed his kit into a regulation-issue duffel.
Three shirts.
A battered field notebook.
The soft-pack of cigarettes.
He didn’t have orders yet.
He didn’t care.
He knew where he was going.
Wherever the fighting was hardest.
Wherever the fire was loudest.

Chapter: Border Fire
They dropped him on the border with half a duffel and one paper-thin promise:
"Keep your head down. Keep your rifle clean."
There was always a war somewhere.
This stretch of wasteland just happened to be the one burning today.
Lexen barely had time to find an empty cot in the barracks, wedge his bag under it, and rack his belt knife on the wall.
The air tasted like dust and old metal.
He lit a cigarette from the broken outlet beside the door, took a long pull—
—and the first mortar round hit the outer fence.
The ground jumped under him.
Concrete dust spat from the ceiling.
Shouts rose outside.
Boots pounded past the door.
Lexen dropped the cigarette, drew his knife, and ran into the smoke.
No orders.  No plan.  Just forward.

Visibility was shit.
Gray plumes rolled through the main quad, curling between wrecked cargo pallets and burned-out transports.
Someone screamed orders he couldn't hear.
A shadow rounded the corner ahead of him—gear low, rifle raised.
Lexen saw the insignia first.
Not theirs.
No time.
He drove forward.
Knife up under the chin guard—through the palate, into the skull.
The man spasmed once and dropped.
Lexen tore the shotgun from his chest rig and pulled the sidearm from his thigh.
The shotgun was heavy and familiar.
The sidearm felt like a toy.
No armor. No helmet.
Just speed.
He moved fast, pressing low against the wall, breathing shallow through the dust.

Lexen fired the shotgun one-handed into the first—
blast straight to the gut.
The second raised a rifle.
Lexen squeezed the sidearm—
nothing.
Safety on.

round grazed his temple.
Blood streaked his vision.

Target dropped.

Two hours later, the last echoes of gunfire faded into the burned hills.

And for the first time since boot,

Assignment


The barracks were low-lit. The sky hadn't broken yet, but the rain was close. Somewhere behind the mess tent, two privates argued about a busted drone. Lexen ignored them.


He sat on the edge of the loading dock, undershirt damp with sweat and blood. His shotgun rested across his lap. A rag hung loose from one hand. He’d cleaned half the receiver, then stopped.


Rafe dropped down beside him. No armor. Just the undersuit and a tired flask.


He didn’t speak. Just drank once, then offered it.


Lexen took it this time. No hesitation.

"That was fast work today. You didn’t wait on orders."


"Didn’t hear any."

"No."

When he set it down, his hand lingered on the grip like he was holding something steady inside himself.


The Briefing Tent


The rain hadn’t hit yet, but the air felt heavy with it.


Inside the canvas field tent, the squad lounged in the glow of an old tactical projector.
Map flicker. Crater lines. A shaded red zone labeled GLASSWOOD in block text.

Vale sat cross-legged on a supply crate, balancing a flask on her knee and a tablet in her lap—cycling through drone footage with one finger and cursing under her breath.

Lexen stood near the back, leaning against a support pole, cleaning the receiver of his shotgun rifle with a cut square of cloth. Slow. Exact.


The tension wasn’t thick.
Just quiet.


These weren’t soldiers who needed to fill the silence.
They’d already heard their own voices too many times.

Glasswood


They called it a forest, but the Glasswood was more crater than canopy.
Trees fused into black towers from past kinetic burns.
Every step sank into ash-mud and shattered stone.
No birds.
No insects.
Just boots and breath and the slow grind of Lexen’s machete slicing low brush ahead of the line.


He moved quiet.
Not cautious.
Just efficient.
Didn’t glance back. Didn’t need to.


The others were there.
Rafe behind him—steady breath, rifle up.
Gorrin off to the left flank, heavy-footed but precise.
Vale in the center, her pack humming with the uplink hardware.
Rook sweeping the rear, suppressor screwed on, stimstick clenched between his teeth.


No one cracked jokes now.

The first sign of trouble was the smell—hot metal and burned copper.
Lexen stopped mid-step. Raised a fist.


Rook slowed—
Click.


The pressure mine threw him sideways into a tree. Armor cracked at the ribs.


Gorrin was already moving—dragged Rook out of the kill zone, left arm shielding the worst of the splinters.

"Starting the rip!" she called. "Two minutes to full pull!"

Lexen dropped the shotgun from its magnet clip, turned toward the bark echo, and fired a spread into the nearest fused trunk.


CRACK.


The tree split like thunder. One sniper fell with it, screaming, tangled in limbs and bark.


"Contact left!" Rafe barked over comms. "Lex, forward push—Gorrin, cover the tech!"


Lexen advanced.


Another sniper—wounded but moving—reached for a sidearm.
Lexen released the shotgun, harness pulling tight, and leveled his assault rifle.

Two shapes burst from cover.
Close combatants. Light rigs. Fast.

One round slammed into his shoulder. Armor caught most. Not enough.
Arm went slack. Rifle dropped.

Machete out.

Blood on bark.
Ash underfoot.


Behind him, Vale called, "Download at 87 percent!"

Drone whine—
Fast approach. High-pitched.

Boom. Boom. Boom.

Then stood.

THE SYNERGY


EXT. FORWARD OUTPOST – OUTER RIDGE – DUSK


The sky was bruised purple.


Smoke from a recent strike still curled over the far hills. The outpost sat quiet—too quiet for what they’d just survived.


Lexen sat on a rusted shipping crate, jacket unzipped, boots off, sleeves rolled. His face was smeared with ash. His lips were chapped. His hands trembled just slightly, the way they always did after a mission.


His flask sat beside him. Empty.


A few soldiers gathered near a burn barrel, passing a joint around. Their laughter was low and half-feral—the kind that came after too many close calls and too little sleep.


Rafe peeled away from the group. He had a cigarette tucked behind one ear, sleeve stained with dried blood.


He walked over, casual.


“You look like shit,” he said.


Lexen didn’t look up.
“I’m breathing.”


Rafe sat beside him. Pulled out the joint. Lit it with a match.
Took a long drag. Held it.
Exhaled slow.


Then he offered it, wordless.


Lexen didn’t move at first.


“It’s not a stim,” Rafe said. “It won’t jack your nerves. It’ll just… round the edges.”


Lexen took it.
Sniffed it like it might be a trap.
Took a drag.


He coughed hard.


“Burns different,” he rasped.


“That’s the point.”


He took another hit.
This time, it settled.
Warmth bloomed behind his eyes. A sinking feeling, but not like drowning.


The world softened at the corners.
The blood in his mouth tasted less metallic.
The fire in his chest dimmed—but didn’t die.


“Shit,” he muttered. “This… works.”


Rafe chuckled.
“You get the hang of it. Drink’s for the dark. This is for the after.”


Lexen stared at the joint like it was some kind of relic.
“What happens if you mix ’em?”


Rafe blinked. Then he laughed—low, rough. The kind of laugh that had a story behind it.


“You find a whole new god.”


INT. BUNKER BARRACKS – LATER THAT NIGHT


Lexen lay on a cot, one hand draped over the edge.
Half a flask in his gut. Smoke still on his tongue.


His eyelids were heavy. His body sore. But his mind wasn’t racing.
Not numb. Not wild. Just… open.


He thought of the mission. Of the blood.
He remembered the tree cracking in half.
The weight of the machete.
The look in Vale’s eyes when she’d said they needed a new name for him.


“Reckoner,” someone had said.
He didn’t feel like anything right now but lungs and ash.


He lit a cigarette.
Watched the ember bloom.


“Goddamn,” he whispered. “This might be the only time I’m real.”

Recoil
The firefight lasted under a minute.
Three bodies down in the entryway. One still twitching by the server bank. Rafe was on comms. Vale watched the door.
Lexen moved fast, clearing corridors with practiced rhythm. His shoulder ached. The shotgun was nearly dry.
