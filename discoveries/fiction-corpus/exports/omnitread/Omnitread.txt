CHAPTER 1 — INVITE
Evan Mercer stared at the email.
SUBJECT: Invitation to Closed Beta (Hardware Tier: MAX)
FROM: no-reply@parallax-interactive.net
The body was short. No flashy banner. No fake-hype copy. Just text:
We are seeking high-end VR users with full-motion rigs and haptic peripherals.
Our simulation supports:
– Iris Rift (all models)
– OmniTread and similar omnidirectional platforms
– Wrist and ankle haptics
– Optional chest pad feedback
Features:
– Fully procedural mission generation
– Infinite replayability
– Persistent world state
Click to install.
He checked the sender again. The domain was generic as hell. The footer was nothing but a barely compliant blower line: You are receiving this because you opted in to marketing communications. He almost laughed. He had opted into a lot of things, sure. Most had died quiet deaths in abandoned inbox folders.
He leaned back in his chair and looked over his kingdom of wasted money.
The OmniTread sat in the corner like a giant black coin on a stand, harness folded up like a dead spider. The thing had cost more than his first car. It had exactly two games that used it properly. One was a “gamified cardio experience” that boiled down to jogging in place while an AI shouted encouragement. The other was a sports title that had been abandoned in early access after the devs realized nobody wanted to run wind sprints in their living room.
The Iris Rift headset got more use, but for work. Floating monitors, code windows, simulated multiple screens. The wrist haptics mostly sat on their charging dock. The ankle straps lived in a drawer. The chest pad, a hard plate with embedded motors, leaned against the treadmill mount.
There was a time he’d imagined using all of it at once. Sprinting through fantasy ruins, diving behind cover, feeling bullets whiz by. That… had not materialized.
But this email said: Hardware Tier: MAX.
He clicked the link.
The browser spat up a minimalist landing page. Black background. White logo that could’ve been anything—some abstract cube folded into itself. Three sentences of marketing fluff. No trailer. No feature list. No company history. A tiny DOWNLOAD CLIENT button blinked like something from a decade-old MMO.
“Could be malware,” he muttered.
He hovered a finger over the mouse. Pulled it back. Looked around his silent apartment, lit mostly by the glow of his monitors. No unread messages from friends. No notifications he cared about.
Boredom tugged harder than caution.
“Fuck it.”
He clicked.
A small file dropped into his downloads folder. Suspiciously small. He opened it. A progress bar popped up.
INSTALLING…
 Then immediately: COMPLETE.
“No way,” he said softly.
Either it was about to stream half the game at runtime, or he’d just let the cheapest ransomware on the net into his system.
A new icon appeared on his desktop. PARALLAX. Simple white cube, dark background.
He stared at it for a long ten-count.
He’d spent thousands on hardware. He had a full-body rig that mostly gathered dust. He’d bought into the fantasy of immersion and gotten two glorified tech demos.
His mouse moved on its own. Or it felt that way.
He double-clicked.
The monitors went black. Then:
A forest.
Not a main menu. Not a company logo. Just—forest. Filling both screens. Grainy, like old film. The fidelity was unreal. Leaves moved in wind that he didn’t hear yet. Shafts of light cut through branches. The image had depth. Too much depth for a flat monitor.
He reached automatically for the Iris Rift.
The headset was still warm from his last work session. He slid it down. The world of his office vanished.
The forest swallowed him.
He was standing on a dirt track in a shallow gully, trees climbing up both sides. The “grain” was still there, a faint analog fuzz overlaying everything, but underneath… it was the most convincing real-time environment he’d ever seen.
He looked around.
No menus. No cursor. No “PRESS START.”
He heard birds. Wind. A distant trickle of water.
“Nice screensaver,” he said. His voice sounded small inside the headphones.
He lifted the headset again, feeling for his wrist controllers on the charger dock. Slipped them on. Fingered the triggers. Fastened the ankle bands for good measure. After a second’s hesitation, he grabbed the chest pad and strapped it on, pulling the Velcro tight.
Last, he walked over to the OmniTread.
The platform hummed to life at a voice command, motors waking underfoot. He stepped onto the low concave surface and clipped into the harness. Waist, thighs, shoulders. The crotch strap bit into him, as always.
“Immersion,” he muttered. “At the cost of your balls.”
He settled the headset back down.
The forest stayed.
No calibration. No countdown.
Just him, in the trees.
His hands were there—gloved in black. When he flexed his fingers, the avatar flexed too, haptics buzzing faintly. He looked down. Black jeans. Black long-sleeve shirt. Black gloves. He could see the faint edge of a mask intruding at the sides of his vision, just barely.
“Edgy,” he said.
He took a tentative step.
The OmniTread responded like it had been waiting its whole life for this. The platform slid under his feet, offering just enough friction to feel like ground, motors adjusting with subtle shifts of tilt. The in-game camera tracked with his movement, perfectly synced. No lag. No drift.
He took another step. Then another.
No joystick. No teleport. Just walking.
He moved his feet faster. The platform tilted upward, mimicking an uphill incline. The gully walls rose around him. Dirt and small rocks crunched underfoot. Leaves brushed his shoulders, invisible in his apartment, real in his ears.
“Okay,” he said quietly. “You got my attention.”
After a minute of wandering, nothing happened.
No objective markers. No narration. Just trees, wind, birds.
He started to wonder if this really was just some kind of interactive benchmark.
Then a voice spoke in his ear.
“Proceed to the GPS coordinates on your HUD.”
The voice was deep. Too deep. Artificially pitched down and filtered, as if someone had taken an ordinary voice and forced it to sound like god.
A compass appeared at the top of his vision. A small glowing blip pulsed on the edge, slightly to the left, pointing uphill.
HUD. Voice-of-god tutorial. He relaxed a fraction. Finally. A game.
He started walking toward the marker.
As he climbed out of the shallow creek bed, the OmniTread adjusted its pitch, creating the illusion of uphill push. His leg muscles responded automatically, bracing harder. The system met him halfway, matching his speed and stride so precisely it erased the old familiar disconnect he’d had in every previous game.
He looked down as he walked.
His feet were perfect.
Every step landed on dirt, exposed roots, rocks. No clipping. No half-sunk toes or ankle-deep grass cheats. His legs bent properly on uneven surfaces. His shoes didn’t jitter in place when he stopped.
Inverse kinematics, sure. Plenty of games had that now. But this felt dialed-in to an absurd level. Somebody had put in work.
He crested the rise, breathing a little harder despite himself.
Below, in a shallow valley, sat a house.
Not a decrepit haunted cabin. A house. Modern. Clean lines. Big windows. Stone and wood and glass. A stream ran past the property, crossed by a small wooden bridge. The evening light turned the water gold.
“Infiltrate the house and locate the room on the second floor marked on your HUD,” the deep voice said.
His vision overlaid icons.
Cone-shaped wedges of faint light appeared, sweeping back and forth from cameras mounted under the eaves and along the property line. Dots glowed where guards patrolled. Outside only; the HUD clearly indicated NO INTERNAL COVERAGE with a small warning. Annotations floated over windows: GLASS BREAK SENSORS. CONTACT ALARMS.
It wasn’t hand-holding. It was a tactical overlay. Clean. Professional.
“Security cameras and exterior guards are highlighted for you,” the voice continued. “Interior guards are not, due to line-of-sight limitations. There are no internal cameras, but the security system is active.”
Evan frowned. “So… stealth. Great.”
He was a decent stealth player. But not a patient one. His natural instinct in most games was to sneak until it failed, then solve everything with violence.
He looked at his HUD. No weapon icons. No inventory.
“Unarmed. Love that for me,” he murmured.
He made his way down the slope, keeping as low as he could. There was no crouch button here—no quick toggle. The OmniTread harness didn’t love deep squats, but he managed a low, careful lope, trusting the treadmill to catch every step.
He skirted trees and bushes, staying just outside the glowing camera cones. The guards’ positions updated in real time as they walked their paths.
One was closer than the others, standing alone near a koi pond on the edge of the manicured lawn. His guarding, such as it was, consisted of looking at fish.
Classic.
Evan threaded through the last line of trees and stopped at the grass line. The guard’s back was to him, posture relaxed. Intel marker over his head confirmed: GUARD. ARMED.
He’d done this a thousand times in other games. Sneak up. Choke out. Drag body into shadow. Easy.
He stepped off the dirt and onto the carefully cut lawn, keeping his steps light. The sound design was absurd—subtle difference between dirt and grass underfoot, different vibration patterns through the OmniTread.
He closed the distance.
Ten feet.
Five.
Two.
He lunged, wrapping his gloved hands around the guard’s neck from behind, ready for the usual animation to snap into place and do the rest.
Nothing snapped.
The man exploded backward.
His head slammed into Evan’s faceplate. The chest pad and headset both detonated with a brutal haptic buzz that made white flare across Evan’s vision. The impact rocked the OmniTread under him. His grip broke.
He stumbled. The guard spun on him.
Not a generic stealth NPC. A man with scruff, crow’s-feet, a faint scar along his jaw. Close enough to smell imaginary aftershave.
“What the—”
The first punch took him in the cheek. The world lurched. The second hammered into his jaw. He tried to bring his hands up. The wrist haptics buzzed as fists collided with forearms, simulating impact.
He had played VR boxing games. This was not that. This was nastier. Sharper. Less forgiving.
He managed to block a third strike. The guard shifted weight, chambered a kick.
The push kick hit him center mass.
The chest pad went off like a riot shield slam. The OmniTread pitched under the force vector. For a second he lost his footing entirely and only the harness kept him from going down. In the game, his avatar slammed onto his back, air blasting out of his lungs with an audio grunt.
He rolled on instinct, trying to scramble up, hands searching for purchase that didn’t exist.
The guard stepped in, raising his gun.
It looked huge from this angle. Black metal, bored muzzle, perfectly modeled rifling. The HUD helpfully tagged it: 9MM. LIVE FIRE.
That text made something cold slide down into his stomach.
The guard kept the gun steady with one hand and, with the other, yanked a radio from his shoulder.
“Control, this is East—”
Evan lunged.
He went for the gun, like an idiot. No disarm training. No plan. Just panic.
For one frozen second, he saw his own black-gloved hand reaching up, fingers about to close over the barrel.
Then the world became sound.
The pistol roared. The chest pad and headset both slammed him with a single overwhelming burst of vibration that felt nothing like a rumble pack. It felt like impact. Like his head had been struck with a bat.
Everything went white.
Then black.
No “You Died.”
No respawn timer.
No loading screen.
Just nothing.
CHAPTER 2 — PERSISTENCE
The forest blinked back in.
No fade.
No animation.
Just an instantaneous snap to the same starting spot.
Evan ripped off the headset, chest heaving.
His heart hammered like he’d sprinted a mile.
“Holy shit…”
He stepped off the OmniTread carefully—like it might tilt again and throw him. His knees trembled. His palms were slick. His whole body felt weirdly light, electrified.
He crossed to the mini-fridge, yanked it open, and grabbed a cold Aether Energy. Half the can was gone before he even registered the flavor.
Black cherry.
He pressed the can to his forehead and breathed, forcing his pulse lower.
That had been brutal.
Not video-game brutal.
Not “good haptics” brutal.
That had been real in a way games weren’t supposed to manage.
And despite himself, a grin tugged at his mouth.
He loved it.
He rotated his neck slowly. The helmet’s impact had left a deep ache behind his eyes. His jaw felt bruised. If he were ten years older, the OmniTread’s throw might’ve snapped something.
He wiped his face with his sleeve and went back.
This time he clipped fully into the OmniTread harness—waist, thighs, shoulders. The crotch strap bit as usual, the price of immersion.
The headset came down.
The forest swallowed him again.
No menu.
No options.
No feedback form.
Just trees, wind, and the faint analog grain overlaying everything.
He didn’t waste time. He jogged toward the hill, retracing the route toward the house. His breath synced easily with the treadmill’s pitch adjustments. Sweat prickled at his forehead as he crested the rise—
Then he froze.
Every guard.
Every camera.
Every glowing cone of vision was directed at a single point on the lawn.
Where he had died.
He slowed to a cautious creep.
Moving through the trees, he slipped between camera arcs and eased close enough to see the scene clearly.
A body in black. Masked. Sprawled near the koi pond.
His body.
His stomach lurched. The unreality of it hit harder than the guard’s kick ever had. Games didn’t leave corpses behind. This felt… wrong. Like a rule had been broken somewhere deep under the surface.
Guards stood around his corpse with guns drawn. One nursed a bright welt on his neck—Evan’s doing. Another whispered urgently into a phone. A third scanned the treeline, tense.
Persistence.
He’d joked about it in procedural roguelites.
He hadn’t expected it to mean this.
But right now, the “persistent corpse” was also a distraction. A gift.
He slipped around the far side of the property, staying low, weaving through the camera blind spots. A bay window gave him a flash of movement inside.
A man in a suit—well-dressed, polished—approached the glass door.
Perfect.
Evan sprinted.
The OmniTread whirred smoothly beneath him, correcting his stride. Before the man could close the door, Evan launched into a leaping throat punch.
He caught a half-second of the man’s face—realistic in a way that made his skin crawl. Not a guard. A civilian. Homeowner, maybe. Genuine fear widened the man’s eyes.
Impact landed like a hammer.
The man folded. Evan followed, stomping fast and hard. The haptics buzzed differently—lighter, trembly—simulating soft tissue giving way underfoot.
The gore wasn’t stylized.
It wasn’t exaggerated.
It was just… there.
 Familiar and sickening.
He almost gagged.
Almost.
But the first guard had taught him something: hesitation got you killed.
His avatar left bloody footprints on the polished hardwood as he followed the HUD marker upstairs.
The mission room was a study—mahogany desk, leather chair, a warm lamp casting golden light. It looked expensive. Lived in.
The voice returned.
“Take the flash drive and folder on the desk.”
No puzzle. No challenge. Just a command.
Fine by him.
He grabbed the items. The moment they went into his pockets, shouting erupted downstairs.
“Exfiltration route compromised. Seek alternate exit.”
A new waypoint flared bright red.
“Oh, shit-shit-shit—”
This was where, in a normal stealth game, he’d grab a rifle from a guard and go loud. But he didn’t have a gun. Or armor. Or literally anything.
He sprinted to the window. One side had an overhang—a survivable drop if he landed right.
Maybe.
He grabbed the ergonomic desk chair. Its molded grips fit perfectly against his palms; the haptics gave little pulses mimicking weight distribution. Strange how the game made objects feel real but not heavy.
He slammed the chair into the window.
The glass didn’t crack—it boomed, vibrating up his forearms.
“Come on—come on—”
He swung again. Harder.
Footsteps thundered up the stairs.
Guards shouted.
A fist hammered the door behind him.
He rammed the chair again. The metal bent. Cracks spidered through the glass.
He flipped the lock just in time before another violent slam rattled the frame.
He lifted the chair overhead and smashed it down with everything he had.
The window finally burst outward.
He half-stumbled through, nearly overshooting the ledge. His boots skidded on the overhang’s shingles, the OmniTread pitching sharply to simulate the slip. He caught himself with a panicked scrabble.
Gunfire erupted behind him.
Haptic buzz hit along his ribs and shoulder—simulated near-misses, sharp and insistent. One grazed him with a hot sting that sent a full-body shiver through the chest pad.  He hadn't even realized the pad could simulate temperature.
He didn’t look back.
He ran.
The exfil waypoint pulsed brighter, leading him toward the main road. The OmniTread responded beautifully—tilt, pitch, pressure shifts all syncing with the uneven ground. His breath came ragged.
A sleek self-driving SUV rolled into view, headlights washing over him like stage lights.
The door popped open.
He dove inside.
Darkness swallowed everything.
For a moment, he just breathed in the black.
Shaking.
Trying to understand whether he’d won…
or survived.
A single line of white text floated in the void:
MISSION ACCOMPLISHED


CHAPTER 3 — ECHOES


Evan woke up sore the next day.


Not “slept weird” sore.
Full-body wrecked sore.


Calves tight from sprinting.
Ribs bruised from simulated impacts.
Shoulders achey from bracing.
Even his crotch complained—harness rub and sudden jolts weren’t forgiving.


He stretched, winced, muttered:


“Worth it.”


He padded into the kitchen and set the moka pot on the counter. Grinding beans. Filling chambers. Heat on. All muscle memory.


Today’s bag: Rwanda. Fruity. Bright. Better than anything waiting for him at work.


While the pot hissed, he showered. Hot water did nothing for the soreness. He toweled off, stepped into the cold apartment, and sucked on his no-nic vape. The artificial buzz hit fast, then vanished just as quickly.


His reflection in the microwave made him pause.
A faint bruise along his cheek.
A shadow along his jawline.


He’d been hit yesterday.
He’d been killed yesterday.


His brain tried to rise into panic.
He smothered it under routine.


Coffee time.


He poured the moka pot into a big mug and inhaled deeply. It almost, almost made him forget.


First sip—too hot.
Second sip—still too hot.
He drank anyway.


But as soon as the warmth spread through him, the simulation crept back into his head like a dream that refused to fade.


The guard’s face when he lunged.
The thud of the body hitting the ground.
The sickening realism of each stomp.
His own corpse lying in the grass when he respawned.


He swallowed and forced his attention elsewhere.
Coffee. Clothes. Tracksuit. Work.


He drained the mug in three long pulls.


He didn’t feel awake.
He felt… waiting.




---


He slipped on his Iris Rift—desktop mode—and let his workspace software take its time booting. The HUD hung open like a warehouse window. Normally he’d pin Redflix shows to the side to stay conscious.


Today nothing held his attention.


Nile Shipping’s anomaly logs flickered open at last. Thirteen fully automated warehouses under his oversight. Thousands of robots doing quiet, endless, perfect labor.


A blinking icon waited halfway down the screen.


Anomaly detected — Unit G3, Warehouse 8
Description: “Unexpected shelf motion detected.”
Status: Pending human review.


He clicked.


The feed showed a shelf trembling slightly—probably a forklift passing or a misaligned stabilizer.


He flagged it as false movement.


Next anomaly.
Next feed.
All trivial.


Fifteen minutes later, he was done.
That left seven hours and forty-five minutes of pretending his job required a human brain.


He flipped between robot feeds.
Grippers. Lifters. Stackers.
Everything moving with mechanical precision.


Nothing.
Nothing to challenge him.
Nothing to engage him.


He stared at the screens and felt hunger under his breastbone.


For what the simulation had given him.
For danger.
For intensity.
For clarity.


And for a moment—tiny, ugly—he remembered the homeowner’s face as he stomped him.
His stomach twisted.


He shoved the feeling aside.
Work. Just work.




---


His doorbell chimed an hour later—grocery drop-off. He pulled off the headset and collected bags from the step.


No delivery worker.
No hello.
No goodbye.
Just contactless efficiency.


He wasn’t sure he missed anything.


He put the food away. Eggs. Milk. Vat-bacon. The “bacon” sizzled too loud when he fried it, filling the kitchen with synthetic soy smoke. Tasted fine. Felt empty.


He ate mechanically.
Vaped mechanically.
Returned to his desk mechanically.


The malaise settled deeper.


He didn’t want to watch anything.
Didn’t want to game.
Didn’t want to scroll.
Didn’t want anything except—


His eyes drifted to the OmniTread.


He looked away too fast.




---


The afternoon crawled.


Another anomaly. False.
A sandwich. Flavorless.
More vaping.
More robots.
More nothing.


When 5:00 finally arrived, relief hit him so hard it felt like stepping out of a sensory deprivation tank.


He logged out like a man escaping his own life.


Stretch. Pop of joints.
Aether Energy—watermelon lime.
Frozen lasagna into the microfryer.
Another vape hit—sharper this time.
The promise of something electric waiting in the other room.


The lasagna burned his tongue. He didn’t care.
He shoveled it down, wiped his mouth with the back of his hand.


His heartbeat quickened.


He wasn’t even pretending anymore.


He crossed the apartment in long strides—


—and practically ran for the OmniTread.
CHAPTER 4 — ALL OUT ACTION
Evan spawned in as… a webcam.
His vision didn’t follow his head. It was fixed, locked, pointed at a man in an expensive suit sitting before him. The framing cut off at the tie—deliberate, clinical. The room was dim, a single shaft of light carving the man out of darkness.
The deep, distorted voice vibrated through the feed.
“You did well in recovering the data. However, your termination of Unit 2174 has caused… unforeseen consequences.”
Unit 2174.
The civilian he’d killed.
It hadn’t been random.
The game had noticed.
“Therefore,” the voice continued, “this mission will be different. Paramilitary forces will be closing in on 2174’s prototypes. You must recover the prototypes before they do.”
The man reached offscreen and lifted an assault rifle. Sleek. Industrial. Prototype-clean.
“This is the ZR-74. You will not have time to become accustomed to it during the mission. I will demonstrate its operation.”
He walked through each motion—safety, magazine, chambering. Evan didn’t need the tutorial, but the calmness of the demonstration unnerved him more than the information.
Then the man pointed the rifle at the webcam.
“Zone is hot. Be ready.”
He fired.
The world dissolved into white.
—
Evan fell through darkness into a night sky roaring around him. Wind howled. A HUD marker glowed bright and urgent on a building far below.
He looked down. Full gear: flak jacket, heavy pistol, knife, a rifle strapped tight, and a wingsuit stretched between his limbs. Even the airflow around his body translated through the haptics in jittery vibrations.
No flight tutorial.
Just survival.
Below—balcony approaching too fast.
He spotted a parachute cord buried in his gear. Yanked it.
The chute detonated open, buzzing his shoulders violently. His descent slowed, but not enough. He slammed into the wall, scrambled, caught the balcony rail with both hands, and rolled onto the platform.
A sudden gust threatened to drag him right back off. He fumbled with the buckles until the chute tore free and fluttered into the night.
The voice returned.
“Take the metal disk from your pack. Place it here. It will act as a mobile spawn point. It will not function indoors.”
Evan found the disk, placed it. It unfolded like a metallic flower, then shimmered out of sight. Cloaked.
He tried the balcony door.
Locked.
Fine.
He drew his pistol, aimed, squeezed the trigger—
Nothing.
Safety. He flicked it.
There was no zoom when he raised the weapon. No tightening of his view. Instead, a small barrel-cam feed appeared in the lower corner of his vision—crisp, immediate, angling exactly with the muzzle. A tactical periscope welded into his eyes.
He fired.
The blast hammered through his chest and arms. Glass exploded inward. The alarm wailed.
He holstered the pistol, raised the rifle. Another barrel cam flickered in. Clean. Precise.
He stepped inside.
A private office. Money, but not executive money. A study lamp still glowing. Papers scattered. Ordinary in a way that made it feel real.
The HUD marker pointed down.
He cleared corners, relying on the barrel cam—sliding the rifle across angles to see before he exposed himself. It felt… unfair. Like cheating. Like a predator’s sense instead of a human’s.
That’s how he found the first guard.
Vest. Slacks. Standard corporate security. Not a PMC. Not armed for war.
Evan hesitated only long enough to feel the familiar spike of guilt—
Then he fired.
One round.
Right through the nose.
The man folded instantly.
His radio crackled.
“Tom? Gunshots. You there?”
Gunshots?
Plural?
Automatic bursts echoed from below. And a muffled explosion. Grenade?
This wasn’t a stealth mission.
It was a race.
Evan moved toward the prototype marker. Fewer guards than expected. Too few.
When he reached the mezzanine, he understood why.
Below him, a full-on firefight tore through the lobby. Security guards pinned behind shredded furniture. PMC operators firing in disciplined bursts, advancing methodically.
A dark cylinder arced across the room.
“Grenade!” someone shouted.
A guard dove out, kicked it—
It detonated harmlessly mid-roll.
He took bullets for the effort.
Another dragged him behind cover.
The PMC’s flanker swept in from the side.
The guards didn’t stand a chance.
Evan crouched behind the mezzanine railing.
They were perfectly clustered.
He flipped the rifle to full auto, aimed at shin level, and swept the burst across the group. Armor protected torsos. Not legs.
Cries. Falls. Chaos.
One soldier dodged and reached for his radio.
Evan drew his pistol, aligned the barrel cam, and fired one clean shot through the soft gap between helmet and vest.
The body dropped mid-word.
“Tango squad, do you copy? Delta, go check on Tango.”
More squads.
More boots.
A door behind him slammed open.
He spun.
PMC troopers on his level.
Too close.
He raised his rifle.
Click.
Empty.
“Fuck—”
Gunfire ripped into him. Haptics detonated across his ribs, chest pad, hands, ankles. The treadmill jolted violently to simulate losing balance.
Everything snapped to black.
Instant.
Absolute.
Like someone unplugged his soul.
CHAPTER 5 — CORPSE RUN
Evan respawned on the balcony exactly where he’d placed the disk.
With nothing.
No rifle.
No armor.
No pistol.
Not even the knife.
“Aww, come on.” He patted himself down harder, as if gear might magically appear. “Seriously?”
The voice was perfectly calm.
“Your equipment is my property. It has protections. You will need to recover it to complete the mission.”
Protections?
What, did his guns have forcefields?
He didn’t stop to think about it.
He remembered the pistol on the guard he'd killed earlier and sprinted.
Being unarmed here felt like being naked in traffic.
He dropped beside the corpse, grabbed the pistol—just a cheap 9mm, civilian grade. No smart systems. No syncing.
The moment he lifted it, he saw the truth:
No barrel cam.
No overlay.
No guidance.
Just a gun.
Just him.
He swallowed and moved.
The building rattled with distant automatic fire—PMC squads sweeping the floors. His pulse spiked. One mistake now and the corpse-run turned into a corpse-loop.
He crept through the cubicles, using every bit of cover he could find.
Boots.
Multiple pairs.
Getting closer.
He slid under a desk, heart pounding against his ribs.
Flashlight beams washed across ceiling tiles.
Rifles swept the hall.
The squad didn’t bother checking cubicles—they were hunting something else. Hunting hard.
When they moved on, Evan didn’t waste a second. He slipped out, keeping low, following the HUD marker toward his corpse.
And stopped cold.
Now he understood “protections.”
His avatar’s body lay sprawled near the railing—helmet still on, mask still in place.  Untouched.
The burnt PMC slumped beside him was worse—hair singed, skin cracked. Like he’d been struck by lightning at point-blank range.
Another soldier stood guard over both bodies.
Silent.
Ready.
Waiting for extraction.
Guarding Evan’s gear.
His mouth went dry.
The open lobby between them was a killing field. With the barrel cam he could’ve sniped the guard clean. With this dumb pistol… odds were bad.
He scanned desperately.
A heavy metal paperweight sat on a desk nearby. He grabbed it.
Waited for the guard to turn.
And hurled it.
He forgot about the strength boost this world gave him. The paperweight launched like a cannon round and slammed into the far stairwell with a deafening clang.
The guard snapped toward the noise instantly—rifle lifted, posture perfect, attention locked.
His back wide open.
Evan crept forward, pistol leveled. One yard.
Half a yard.
Close enough to see the fabric weave on the vest.
He exhaled, squeezed—
The guard turned.
The round tore along the edge of his neck, carving a groove but not dropping him. Blood sprayed. The guard spun toward Evan with a furious snarl, rifle rising fast.
No time to aim.
No time to think.
Evan lunged, ducking under the barrel just as it barked. Rounds tore overhead, haptic buzz jolting his forehead and ribs.
He hit the guard shoulder-first.
Hard.
The impact wasn’t elegant—it was desperate.
The guard staggered backward, boots skidding on tile, arms pinwheeling.

And as if summoned by it—
A metallic crunch thundered through the night.
Then gunfire.
Then screaming.
The wall shook.
Spooks froze mid-keystroke.
Dylan drew his gun.
Evan rose to his feet, pulse hammering so hard it blurred his vision.
He was here.
The handler had found them.


CHAPTER 13 — CONFRONTATION


Dylan crept toward the window and flicked up the auto-blinds.


He froze.


“Holy… what is that?”


Evan swallowed hard. “It’s the body I helped him steal.”


Below, the mutant carved through federal defenders like they were made of paper. Each pull of the cannon-sized shotgun erupted into a blossom of gore. Armored agents vanished in sprays of red mist.


Bullets hammered the creature—armor, scales, exposed skin—but only left chalk-white freckles. No penetration. No recoil. No effect.


No hesitation.


“I have to help them,” Dylan whispered, voice cracking.


“So do I.” Evan tightened his grip on the window frame. “He’s here for me.”


“You’re a witness. It’s my job to—”


“And it’s my responsibility to fix what I did.” Evan’s stare didn’t waver. “We don’t have time to argue. Unless you want to handcuff me and offer me up as bait.”


Dylan didn’t answer.


He didn’t have to. The silence said it all.


“I don’t have a spare weapon,” he muttered instead.


Spooks cleared his throat from behind them.


“Well…” he said, reaching into his coat pocket, “I might.”


He produced a matte metal block, rectangular and unassuming.


“What is that supposed to be?” Evan asked.


“Japanese SWAT tech.” Spooks sounded almost proud. “Monomolecular edge. Vibro-oscillation. Cuts titanium.”


“You’re shitting me.”


Spooks clicked a button.


The metal unfolded into a curved katana — shimmering, vibrating, humming like it had its own heartbeat.


Evan stared. “A collapsible monomolecular katana?”


Spooks tapped the folded block against his palm. “Never leave home without one. Always bring a sword to a gunfight.”


Evan lifted the blade. It weighed almost nothing — a razor made of air and intent.


They rushed outside.


Smoke drifted like fog. Sirens pulsed. Metal rang. Men screamed.


They ducked behind a shattered SUV just in time to witness a lone defender fire into the mutant’s face. Three shots missed; the fourth hit — a direct strike at the corner of the left eye.


The mutant staggered.
Then roared.


It pulled the trigger—


CLICK.


Empty.


Prototype weapon. Prototype ammo. No pouches. No reload.


Evan registered it instantly.


The mutant grabbed the lone defender and hurled him into a squad car. The entire frame buckled on impact. Glass exploded outward. The agent folded in half with a stomach-churning crunch.


Dylan fired to draw its attention—


The mutant turned.


Coiled.


And leaped.


“MOVE!” Evan shouted.


They dove apart as a fist like a sledgehammer cratered the pavement where they’d been standing.


Dylan sprinted left.


Evan sprinted right.


The mutant locked onto Evan and advanced, each step a seismic shock.


Then the handler’s voice boomed from the creature’s warped throat — distorted, cracked, but unmistakable:


“I had such hopes for you, Evan.”


The creature drew its fist back.


A massive tell.
A VR boss windup telegraph.


“You were exquisite in the simulation,” the handler snarled. “Obedient. Efficient.”


The punch fired like a piston—


Evan sidestepped and raised the blade.


Knuckles met vibrating steel.


SCHLNK—


Half the mutant’s hand fell away, spraying dark magenta blood in a fan across the asphalt.


The creature stared at its ruined fingers — shock, disbelief, fury radiating through its monstrous frame.


“No… no, this form is superior. You should be kneeling — not harming me.”


Then it charged.


Full commitment.
Freight-train momentum.
Zero feints.
Overconfidence.


Evan saw the tell — a compensatory shift of weight the wrong direction, a micro-stutter born from controlling an unfamiliar body.


A VR telegraph.


He pivoted and slashed across the ribs.


The blade carved a deep, wine-dark trench. Magenta blood gushed thick and heavy.


A gunshot cracked — Dylan, precise — punching into the exposed seam of flesh.


The mutant staggered violently, limbs spasming.


Evan recognized the movement instantly:


Stagger state.
Vulnerable window.
Punish opportunity.


“STOP—STOP INTERFERING—” the handler roared, the creature’s throat glitching with distortion. “THIS BODY—ITS NERVES—IT IS OVERRIDING—MY CONTROL—”


Evan didn’t hesitate.


He struck behind the leg — severing the hamstring.


Another shot from Dylan blew out the kneecap.


The mutant crashed to one knee, snarling, half-broken but still reaching for Evan with its remaining hand.


“You were meant to stand beside me,” it gasped. “Not… against me.”


Evan stepped back, centered the blade, breath steady.


Then he lunged.


No windup.
No tell.
A clean, perfect thrust — the kind VR drills hammered into reflex.


The blade slid into the creature’s heart with horrifying ease.
Resistance — then none.
A catastrophic yielding of flesh around vibrating steel.


The handler tried to speak.


“You were…”
A shudder.
“…my finest…”
A convulsion.
“…cre—”


The sentence died with the body.


Magenta blood poured as the mutant collapsed sideways, weight hitting the ground like a fallen tree.


Evan yanked the blade free and stepped back, steadying his breath as the creature’s final spasms twitched out.


He wiped the sword clean in the grass.


The trembling in his hands wasn’t fear.


It was adrenaline.


Resolve.


A new, unfamiliar hardness.


The safehouse door burst open behind him.


Spooks stumbled out, pale, clutching his laptop.


“We… we’ve got him,” he stammered.


CHAPTER 14 — CONVOY


A Blackhawk met them outside the house.
No one argued about Evan coming along.
Not after what he’d done.
Not after what they’d all seen.


The National Guard had already been deployed.
This wasn’t covert anymore—
this was war.


A gruff sniper sat across from Evan, expression granite-hard. Without a word, he shoved gear toward him: a vest, spare mags, a high-caliber magnum, and a parachute pack.


“Strap in,” he growled. “You’ll need it.”


He checked the gun before holstering it.  It was big.  Really big.


“.50 cal magnum.  Kicks like a horse.  Use the laser.”


Evan nodded, fingers trembling—not from fear, but from the leftover adrenaline that still buzzed through his bones. The sword from Spooks lay across his lap, impossibly light and humming faintly with monomolecular vibration.


Spooks hadn’t asked for it back.
Maybe he didn’t expect it to return.


Dylan sat beside Evan, jaw tight, hands clasped around his rifle. He opened his mouth once, then closed it. Tried again. Swallowed.


Evan stared at him. “Spit it out.”


Dylan leaned toward the mic over the roar of the rotors.
“By my count, that’s the second time you’ve saved my life. I… owe you now. It’s a weird feeling.”


“You didn’t get saved,” Evan said. “You helped take that thing down. Couldn’t have done it alone.”


“You’re exaggerating. I saw how you moved. He never stood a chance.”


Evan didn’t respond.


Because Dylan was right.


The intruder’s movements—
the mutant’s telegraphs—
they were obvious to him.
Highlighted.
Predicted.
As if he’d practiced against them a thousand times in a place his conscious mind didn’t remember.


He didn’t like that feeling.


“Those shots into the wounds helped more than you think,” Evan said quietly. “They stunned him. Slowed him. That mattered.”


Dylan grunted, but something in him relaxed.


Below, more helicopters converged with theirs—shadows cutting across the distant glow of city lights. Armored personnel carriers rumbled across the roads like steel beetles.


For a moment, Evan allowed himself to breathe.


Then an APC hit a mine.


The explosion ripped the night open. Fire blossomed upward, tossing the carrier onto its side like a toy. Men scrambled. Some didn’t.


“Jesus—” Dylan muttered.


Something streaked up from the treeline—
a rocket-propelled flare of smoke and fire.


“Incoming RPG!” the copilot barked.


The missile slammed into a helicopter two formations over. Flames swallowed its tail rotor, spinning it wildly before it dropped out of sight.


The radio erupted with panic.


“—taking heavy fire—”
“—multiple launchers—”
“—we need suppressive—”


The sniper beside Evan leaned out the open door, rifle braced on a knee.
One clean shot.
The rifle barked.


“Got the bastard,” he said. “Whoever fired that was a she, for what it’s worth.”


Evan’s stomach dipped.
It clicked instantly.


If she was firing rockets—


“Other players,” he whispered.


His pulse spiked.
Hundreds of printed bodies.
Thousands, maybe.
Controlled by others who thought they were just playing a game.


“Spooks!” Evan shouted into the comms. “You’ve got to shut down the simulation!”


“I can’t do it from here!” Spooks crackled back. “I need a hard line!”


Dylan slammed a mag into his rifle. “Then we get him one.”


Another warning shrieked over the intercom.


“Incoming—two o’clock—MOVE!”


Evan saw it—
Another rocket arcing straight toward them.
Too close.
Too fast.


“JUMP!” the sniper roared, lunging for the door.


Evan didn’t think.
Didn’t plan.
Just moved.


He dove out of the Blackhawk with Dylan and Spooks beside him, the rotor wash ripping at their limbs.


Gravity punched him in the gut.


Wind howled.


The chopper exploded above them—
a sunburst of fire and shrapnel.


Evan’s heart slammed into his skull.


No respawns.


No spawn beacon.


Just sky.


And below:


Hell.


CHAPTER 15 — BOOTS ON THE GROUND




Bullets traced the air around them. Tracers buzzed past like hornets. One round punched straight through Dylan’s chute two yards above the ground. His descent accelerated violently — he hit in a crouch, rolled, and fought the tangled straps.


Evan flared his chute, dropped the final meters hard, and tore himself free just as another burst screamed overhead. The landing rattled him, but he was upright.


He saw the shooter a heartbeat later.


No mask this time — an old man in a hunting jacket, white beard, braced behind a rock with a scoped rifle.


He fired with calm precision.


Evan lifted the magnum with both hands — breath shallow, wrists tight.


BOOM.


The recoil shocked up his arms like a hammer. The top of the old man’s skull vanished in a red mist. His body slumped sideways, boneless and silent.


For a moment, Evan swayed.


That hadn’t been VR. That hadn’t been simulated gore. It was… real.


He swallowed hard, forcing his mind back online.


Spooks was tangled in his chute twenty yards away. Evan sprinted over, slashed him loose, and dragged him behind cover.


Then he heard it.


A faint, insectile buzz under the gunfire.


He snapped toward the noise.


There — behind a rock — something shimmered, half-visible.


A half-formed body was being printed. White bone lattice extruding. Wet, pale skin knitting over it. Featureless limbs twitching with puppetlike spasms.


The disk beneath it flickered into full visibility while printing.


Evan charged it.


He stomped the spawner with all his weight. It shattered, sparks coughing upward. The unfinished body collapsed in a wet slump, melting into unshaped tissue.


“They decloak while printing!” Evan yelled. “Find the hum — smash the disk! They’ll respawn forever if you don’t!”


“Copy!” Dylan shouted back.


A bullet cracked past Evan’s cheek. He dove behind a slab of fallen concrete, heart pounding.


He peeked.


A teenage girl this time. Maybe sixteen. Skinny, empty-eyed. Holding a full-length assault rifle far too large for her frame — but her stance was perfect. Mechanically perfect.


She raked the landscape with fire. Evan threw himself sideways, VR-trained instincts firing. A round grazed his shoulder pad; the chestpad buzzed painfully.


He hit the dirt, rolled, came up behind cover.


Before he could fire, Dylan’s rifle barked twice. The girl jerked, collapsed wordlessly, limbs folding like someone pulling the plug.


Evan approached, cautious.


Her rifle gleamed on the ground — high-end, advanced.


He reached for it—


A memory slammed into him: the Corpse Run.


The blackened body touching his rifle.  Charred.  Unrecognizable.


He ripped his hand back.


“Don’t touch their weapons!” Evan shouted. “They’re trapped. Pick one up and it’ll kill you.”


Dylan froze mid-reach. “What? How do you know?”


“I saw it in the simulation.  It fried someone completely. That wasn’t a mechanic — it was a failsafe.”


“Jesus.”


Evan keyed his radio. “All units, do NOT recover enemy guns. They’re trapped with lethal countermeasures.”


A grim voice replied, “Already learned that the hard way.”


They pushed toward the road, clearing as they went. More buzzing. More half-seen glimmers.


Another disk — tucked under an overturned sedan.
Another — wedged on a ledge above a drainage ditch.
They smashed both before the bodies fully finished printing.


They linked up with a National Guard squad — six soldiers, filthy, exhausted, moving like they’d been fighting nonstop.


“Friendly?” one asked, weapon raised.


“Dylan Warner, FBI,” Dylan shouted. “Mercer and Spooks — civilian support with intel on clone behavior.”


That earned a wave-in.


Their group grew as more survivors joined. APCs were abandoned — one burned in the distance, another lay on its side, tracks blown off.


A Guard sergeant muttered, “Minefield under everything. Road’s dead. Dirt’s dead. Can’t move anything heavier than a man.”


Of course.
The Handler had designed the whole approach like a killbox.


Gunfire erupted intermittently.


After each firefight, someone would whisper, “Listen—”


And the faint hum would give away another printing disk.


They smashed disk after disk, shattering them before clones fully formed.


They saw repeats.


The old man printed again — Dylan shot him on reflex.
The teenage girl printed again — collapsed the same way.
A mechanic. A preacher. A teenage boy in a uniform.


Some came once.
Some came endlessly.


The horror dulled. The guilt blunted. Evan felt something inside him go frighteningly quiet.


By the time the compound loomed into view — a squat perimeter of concrete and barbed wire — killing had become muscle memory.


“Fence is electrified,” Dylan said.


“Yup,” Evan murmured.


A young Guard specialist stepped forward with a demo pack. “They’ll know where we are if I blow this.”


“They already know,” Evan said. “Satellites, thermal. The only place they can’t see us is inside.”


The kid hesitated, then planted the charge.


They crouched back — mouths open, ears covered.


BOOM.


The explosion tore the fence inward. A plume of fire and smoke rolled across them. The barbed wire spat sparks. Sections of chain-link curled like burning paper.


Evan’s hearing went muffled. Dust rained down.


Then—


A deep, seismic THUD rippled through the ground.


Another.


And another.


Not footsteps.


Footfalls.


Evan felt the vibration in his boots, his teeth, the back of his spine.


Floodlights snapped on, one by one, cutting through the smoke.


A massive shape moved beyond the torn fence.


Big.


Wrong.


Not the mutant they’d killed.


Something larger.
Something heavier.
Something built for war.


The soldiers around him froze.


Evan’s fingers tightened on Spooks’s vibrating katana.


He exhaled, low and steady.


“Boss fight,” he whispered.


And the monster stepped into the light.


CHAPTER 16 — BOSS FIGHT




They didn’t have time to absorb the monster’s shape before gunfire erupted again. A fresh wave of clones poured out of the main building, rifles barking.


“CONTACT!” someone shouted.


Evan turned to the demo tech.
“Got another pack? I’m gonna need it.”


“I’ve got C4—here!” The tech slapped a dense block of explosives into Evan’s palm, then shoved a pen-sized detonator after it. Clone fire peppered the ground around them as he frantically demonstrated the safety. “Press here to arm—here to send—DON’T mix them up!”


“I’m up,” Evan said, gripping the charge. “Cover me!”


He sprinted.


The new monster lumbered out of the smoke behind him—massive, misshapen, roided beyond anatomical sense. Fleshy bulges knotted under its slick skin. Hunched, it stood fifteen feet. Straightened, it would scrape twenty.


Huge.
Ugly.
Half-formed.
Power incarnate.


The perimeter speakers crackled alive.


“Hello, Evan,” the Handler boomed. “This one runs an AI assistance package. Much faster than my… meager reflexes.”


The monster bent and wrapped one enormous hand around a steel girder. Like it was picking up a stick.


Evan broke from cover, sprinting toward it. He had to get inside its range—go for the legs—because nothing else on earth would stop something that size.


The girder came down.


Not fast—
but inevitable.


Evan dove aside as the ground exploded where he’d stood. Concrete burst in a geyser. Dylan fired to cover him, his rounds leaving only faint pinpricks across the creature’s thick hide.


The girder hit, stuck for a second—


Then the monster wrenched it sideways.


The sweep tore a trench across the ground, rolling toward Evan like a horizontal freight train. Evan jumped at the last possible heartbeat, boots barely clearing the metal as it screamed under him.


The girder crushed an outbuilding beside him, flattening it into debris.


Evan sprinted directly toward the monster, sword low at his side. The vibrating monoblade hummed with lethal promise. The beast reared back for another smash, but at close range the size became less of an an advantage—its own bulk slowed precise targeting.


He slid under its reach and slashed at the nearest leg.


The blade bit deep.


The monster’s foot surged upward for a stomp—too fast for its size. Evan darted around the outside of the planted leg. The stomp hit where he’d been, the impact rattling his bones even from safety.


But the movement threw the creature off balance—its AI overcorrecting. The planted leg twisted, weight shifting wrong.


Evan struck again.


A clean, brutal carve into the wobbling leg.


Thick, magenta fluid gushed out in sheets.


The monster staggered, lifting its good foot for another stomp—telegraphed, heavy, trackable. Evan rolled aside again, feeling the air compress as the foot cratered the ground behind him.


The beast stepped back, trying to bring its weapon to bear.




Dylan saw it.  The rear leg descending, blood dripping from it.  He had a clean line of fire.


A shot cracked.


The round slammed into the open wound, tearing deeper into the lumpy flesh.


The monster buckled, the foot unable to support its weight.


One knee crashed to the ground, sending tremors through Evan’s boots. Its other hand gripped the girder hard enough to bend metal, using it to hold itself upright.


Perfect.


Evan closed the last few meters in three long strides. Its groin—its lower torso—was now right in reach. He leapt upward, blade flashing.


He drove the monoblade upward, carving a ragged opening into its abdomen.


No cry of pain.
No flinch.
Just mechanical resistance, then give.


He didn’t need it to feel anything.


He needed an opening.


Evan shoved the C4 charge into the cavity, pressing it deep into the pulsing mass.


Then he kicked backward, rolling away hard.


“CLEAR!” he shouted.


He hit the detonator.


CHAPTER 17 — DETONATE


The beast ripped apart.


The explosion didn’t just hit—it erased the thing. Its torso separated from the hips in a wet, catastrophic burst that drenched Evan in hot magenta gore. Chunks of flesh slapped the concrete. A massive hand twitched once, twice… then fell still.


And as one, the clones collapsed.


Like puppets with cut strings.


Evan staggered back, chest heaving, wiping gore from his eyes. Around him, the battlefield went quiet—unnervingly quiet. Bodies lay everywhere, clone and soldier alike, the sudden stillness ringing louder than gunfire.


Near the compound wall, Spooks popped up from behind a busted server cabinet. His laptop was hardwired into a blown-out junction panel.


He lifted a trembling thumbs-up.


“Got access! Server’s offline! Not before I snagged—oh boy—about half his archive. Encryptions nasty, but we’ve got federal-grade toys now.”


Soldiers—battle-hardened, blood-smeared, exhausted—looked at Evan in a new way.
Not fear.
Not confusion.


Respect.


They followed him inside without being told.


The building’s interior was eerily silent now that the respawns were gone. No humming. No printing. No footsteps. Just the heavy thunk of boots on tile and the faint buzz of emergency lights.


They found him in a reinforced control room.


The Handler sat on the floor.


Unarmed.
Legs calmly crossed.
Hands folded in his lap like he’d been meditating.


He didn’t rise to greet them.


When he lifted his eyes to Evan, there was no fear—only appreciation. Almost… pride.


“I’m proud of you, Evan,” he said softly. “Look at what I made you into. A true warrior.”


Evan’s grip tightened on the bloody monoblade. His voice came out low, steady.
“You didn’t make me anything. Except a murderer. I learned everything I needed before you ever found me.”


The Handler’s smile widened—something cold and knowing in it.


“Did you?” he asked.


Evan frowned. “What?”


The Handler tilted his head, expression strangely gentle.


“And what about—”


His tie detonated.


A perfect red ring snapped outward, vaporizing flesh and bone. His head left his shoulders in a clean arc, hitting the floor with a wet thud.


Dylan swore as another wave of gore sprayed him. “Every damn time…”


But Evan hardly heard him.


Because beneath the shock, something else churned in his gut.


The Handler had been relieved.


Smiling.


Almost triumphant.


His half-sentence echoed like a knife turning in Evan’s ribs.


What about what?
What the hell did he know?
What had he started?


Spooks knelt by the corpse, checking the ruined collar.
“Remote trigger,” he said grimly. “Someone else out there hit the kill-switch. Didn’t want him talking.”


Evan stared at the headless body.


The danger wasn’t over.


It had just become bigger.


“Great,” he muttered.

The Handler’s last words.

 Then the explosion had cut him off.

Still.

Listening.
Waiting.
